## INTEGRATION v01
---
integration_mode: BODY-LANDED
reframe_type: BODY-FACT
---
My shoulders have lived at my ears for weeks. The meeting invitations ping. The calendar fills. My breath becomes shallow without my noticing. This is what anxiety in management looks like—not panic attacks in parking lots, but the slow tightening of tissue around bone.

I'm learning something: anxiety is just information. Not a flaw. Not a sign I can't do this. Just data that says: your nervous system is registering threat.

The reframe isn't magical. It's just honest. Yes, this is hard. Yes, I feel it physically. And yes, I continue anyway.

When I notice my shoulders creeping up, I'm not trying to fix the feeling. I'm just acknowledging: there it is. My body knows something matters to me.

**My body speaks what I need to hear.**

By the afternoon, my shoulders drop an inch. The anxiety hasn't left. It's just made space for me to exist alongside it. That space is everything.

## INTEGRATION v02
---
integration_mode: BODY-LANDED
reframe_type: ACCEPTANCE-THROUGH-ACTION
---
I thought managing anxiety meant eliminating it. So I tried harder, controlled more, planned obsessively. My hands stayed tense on the keyboard for eight hours straight.

The truth I'm learning: anxiety doesn't disappear. It transforms.

When I do the grounding exercises, the anxiety stays but my grip on my chair loosens. When I practice the breathing, my mind still spirals but my chest expands. The anxiety remains. I become larger around it.

This is not defeat. This is integration.

**I can be anxious and still move forward.**

My colleague asked yesterday why I looked calmer. I told her: I'm not. I'm just deciding not to let the anxiety make my decisions. That decision, made a hundred times a day, changes everything. Not the feeling. The relationship to the feeling.

## INTEGRATION v03
---
integration_mode: PRACTICAL-GROUNDED
reframe_type: RESOURCE-SHIFT
---
My anxiety wants me to control the future. It whispers: prepare more, plan tighter, anticipate everything.

I've started asking a different question: what can I actually control right now.

This moment, I can control my breath. I can control my posture. I can control whether I send that email or wait five minutes. These small controls give me power that the future-controlling never did.

My body knows the difference. When I'm controlling small, real things, my nervous system settles. When I'm trying to control the uncontrollable future, my jaw clenches.

**I control what's in my hands.**

The reorg announcement still brings anxiety. But now I know: I can't control the reorg. I can control how I show up. I can control what I say to my team. I can control my own steadiness. That's enough.

My breathing is slower today. Not because the anxiety left. Because I stopped fighting what I can't control.

## INTEGRATION v04
---
integration_mode: BODY-LANDED
reframe_type: NORMALCY-REDEFINED
---
I used to think normal meant: no anxiety. No racing heart before meetings. No tightness in my chest during difficult conversations.

I'm learning normal for me is: anxiety and action together.

This is the normal of someone managing teams in uncertain environments. The anxiety says something matters. It says I care. It says my nervous system is working hard to keep me safe. These are not flaws.

My body is doing exactly what it should.

**My anxiety shows I care deeply.**

When the performance review cycle opens and my hands shake while opening my emails, I'm not broken. I'm a manager who cares about people. The anxiety is the price of caring. The price is bearable.

I'm not aiming for calm anymore. I'm aiming for grounded. I can be anxious and grounded. That's the integration. My shoulders are slightly lower today. Not because the anxiety left. Because I stopped believing it meant something was wrong with me.

---
## INTEGRATION v05
---
integration_mode: MIND-SHIFT
reframe_type: SEPARATION
---
I spent so long believing my anxiety reflected reality. If I felt anxious about the project, the project must be doomed. If my heart raced before the all-hands, everything must be terrible.

I'm learning to separate the signal from the certainty.

My anxiety is a signal that something matters. It's not information about what will actually happen. My racing heart before the quarterly business review doesn't predict failure. It predicts that I care about the presentation. That's all.

This separation is revolutionary.

**My feelings predict my investment, not outcomes.**

When I notice the anxiety rising, I'm getting better at asking: what's actually happening versus what's my nervous system assuming. Usually, the reality is much smaller than the anxiety. Usually, I can do the thing.

This morning, before the difficult one-on-one, I felt all the familiar anxiety. I sat with it for two minutes. Then I walked in. The conversation happened. It was hard, not catastrophic. My anxiety was wrong about the outcome. But my anxiety was right that it mattered.

## INTEGRATION v06
---
integration_mode: BODY-LANDED
reframe_type: RHYTHM-RESTORATION
---
I notice the pattern now. My anxiety peaks on Mondays and before meetings I've been dreading. This isn't random. My body knows the calendar.

Instead of fighting the pattern, I'm working with it.

Mondays, I build in buffer time. I don't schedule back-to-back meetings. I eat better. I notice the anxiety without fighting it. By Tuesday afternoon, my nervous system settles. This is the rhythm of my anxiety. Not a flaw in me. Just the rhythm.

The reframe: I can work with my rhythm instead of against it.

**My anxiety has a pattern, and I can honor it.**

Yesterday I built a Monday that supported my anxious system. I left space. I moved slowly. I did grounding exercises. By Tuesday morning, the anxiety was half as loud. My body responded to being worked with, not fought against.

This isn't magical. It's just respect for how I'm wired. The anxiety isn't disappearing. But my relationship to it is becoming sustainable. My shoulders are lower when I'm working with my body instead of against it.

---
## INTEGRATION v07
---
integration_mode: INTEGRATED-ACTION
reframe_type: PRODUCTIVE-ANXIETY
---
I used to see my anxiety as the enemy of my productivity. I tried to be calm, focused, certain. I failed constantly.

I'm learning my best work happens with slight anxiety. The edge of my nervous system activated. Not paralyzed. Just alert.

When I'm calm, I'm lazy. When I'm anxious, I'm thorough. When my heart is slightly elevated, I pay attention to details other people miss. The anxiety has been making me a better manager. I just called it a problem.

The reframe: my anxiety is not a bug, it's a feature of how I work best.

**My sharpness comes from my edges.**

This week, I had to present the budget strategy. I felt the familiar anxiety rising. I sat with it. I let it keep me alert. I caught mistakes in the data before I presented. I asked better questions. The anxiety made the presentation better. Not worse.

I'm not aiming to eliminate the anxiety anymore. I'm aiming to channel it. To let it sharpen my mind without tightening my jaw. To let it make me thorough without making me frozen.

My body knows what it's doing. I'm finally starting to trust it.
