## REFLECTION v01
---
family: F1
voice_mode: teacher
mechanism_emphasis: automatic
tier_variant: C

What I notice is a particular quality of attention that never quite turns off. I walk into the classroom and a part of my nervous system takes a baseline reading: Who absent is beneath the performance. Who looks agitated. What the energy level is becomes the real question. Where is the potential friction. This is not something I choose to do. It happens automatically, a scanning mechanism that runs beneath the conscious lesson plan. The mechanism I am describing is sometimes called anticipatory vigilance. It is the system that learned to read a room so that crisis could be prevented rather than responded to. Over years of managing thirty minds at different developmental stages, it became efficient. It became invisible.

I can have a relatively calm day in the classroom — the lessons went smoothly, the students were focused, no major incidents — and still feel depleted in a way that sleep does not seem to touch. It requires a constant expenditure of cognitive resources. The exhaustion that comes not from what I did but from what I was attending to while I was doing it.

There is a particular texture to the anxiety that emerges when I am communicating with a parent. Not all parents, not always, but when I am drafting an email that conveys something difficult or preparing for a conversation about their child's behavior or performance, something shifts in my nervous system. What I recognize here is the mechanism of asymmetrical accountability. I am responsible for the outcome of my communication. If the parent becomes angry, I have failed to communicate effectively. The responsibility is entirely mine to make the interaction work. This is different from the classroom anxiety.

What I am aware of is a particular kind of anxiety that emerges around competence. Not in the moment of doing the work, but in the reflection afterward. I delivered a lesson. Some students understood. Some did not. I managed the classroom. Some behavioral choices still required redirection. Nothing went wrong. There is no version of the day where every student is fully engaged and learning at their optimal level.
