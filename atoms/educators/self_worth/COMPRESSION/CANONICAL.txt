## COMPRESSION v01
---
compression_family: C1
---
The mechanism of external self-worth in teaching is that you have outsourced the valuation of yourself to test scores, student evaluations, and administrator observations. The test score goes up and you feel valuable. The student complains and you feel worthless. Your mood is entirely tethered to external feedback. You are on a roller coaster run by other people's assessments. The terrible part is it works. You feel genuinely valuable when the metrics say so. You also feel genuinely worthless when they do not.

---

## COMPRESSION v02
---
compression_family: C2
---
The cost of using external validation as a measure of self is that you are never safe. The validation can be withdrawn. The student can give a bad evaluation. The administrator can find fault. The test scores can drop. So you are in a perpetual state of precarity. You must perform constantly because the moment you relax, the moment you show vulnerability, the validation might disappear. You have made yourself dependent on a system you cannot control.

---

## COMPRESSION v03
---
compression_family: C3
---
Your body knows when you do not believe in yourself. It becomes smaller. Your voice becomes less clear. You defer to the administrator even when you have the expertise. You apologize for your teaching choices even when they are sound. The body registers the self-doubt and pulls back. When you actually believe in yourself, the body expands. Your voice carries weight. You speak with authority. The body knows the truth before the mind admits it.

---

## COMPRESSION v04
---
compression_family: C4
---
The relational consequence is that you have made the students responsible for your sense of worth. When they learn, you feel like a good teacher. When they struggle, you feel like a failure. You have given them the power to determine how you feel about yourself. Now they sense this and either withdraw or perform in ways designed to comfort you. The relationship is distorted. They should not have to manage your sense of adequacy.

---

## COMPRESSION v05
---
compression_family: C5
---
The pattern is compulsive: you work to earn validation, the validation comes, you feel temporarily valuable, the validation fades, you feel worthless again, you work harder. The cycle is self-perpetuating. There is no endpoint. There is no amount of validation that will actually fix the underlying belief that you are not worth anything. So you keep working. The work is the problem and the solution. It is eating you from the inside and you cannot stop.

---

## COMPRESSION v06
---
compression_family: C1
---
What low self-worth does is make you a perfectionist about everything. Your lessons must be flawless because if they are flawed, you are flawed. Your grading must be perfect because if there is an error, you are incompetent. Your classroom must be managed perfectly because if there is chaos, you have failed. There is no middle ground. This is the bind. You cannot be perfect. No one can. So you are always failing the test. You are always coming up short.

---

## COMPRESSION v07
---
compression_family: C2
---
The hidden cost is the relationships you have destroyed by trying to earn worth through over-functionality. You say yes to everything. You do all the extra work. You make yourself indispensable through service. But somewhere along the way, the students and parents lose respect for you. They do not want a teacher who needs them to feel valuable. They want a teacher who is valuable independent of what they can provide. So your attempt to earn their respect through service actually reduces it.

---

## COMPRESSION v08
---
compression_family: C3
---
Your nervous system is coded to detect threat and when you have low self-worth, everything feels like a threat. A critical comment is evidence of your inadequacy. A student complaint is proof of your incompetence. A mistake is indictment of your entire being. Your nervous system is overactive because you have told it that criticism is dangerous. It believes you. So it mobilizes. It prepares defenses. It interprets the world as hostile. The threat is not external. It is internal narrative.

---

## COMPRESSION v09
---
compression_family: C4
---
The relational truth is that you are playing a role of someone who is a good teacher and hoping that if you play it well enough, you will eventually become convinced it is true. The performance is exhausting. The internal contradiction is exhausting. You cannot let anyone see you as anything other than competent. So you are always performing. You are never off the stage. The students and parents do not see you. They see the performance. So you are alone while appearing connected.

---

## COMPRESSION v10
---
compression_family: C5
---
The temporal pattern is that your self-worth fluctuates based on the last student interaction, the last parent email, the last administrator feedback. You are not stable. You are not rooted. You are reactive. This means you cannot plan because you do not know who you are going to be depending on what happens. This moment's competence becomes next moment's inadequacy. The oscillation is exhausting. You have no solid ground. You are always falling or temporarily suspended.

---

## COMPRESSION v11
---
compression_family: C1
---
The system that makes low self-worth work is the belief that you can teach your way to being valuable. This is false. You are valuable because you are alive. Not because of what you do in the classroom. But you do not believe this. So you keep teaching. You keep achieving better outcomes. You keep performing. And you never get to the place where you can rest in your own value because you have made value contingent on production.

---

## COMPRESSION v12
---
compression_family: C2
---
The cost accumulates in the ways you harm yourself to prevent being harmed. You reject yourself before anyone else can reject you. You criticize your own teaching before anyone else can criticize it. You do not try new things because the failure might mean you are incompetent. You have become your own worst enemy. You are doing the damage you were trying to prevent others from doing. The self-worth problem has become self-harm.

---

## COMPRESSION v13
---
compression_family: C3
---
Your physical experience of low self-worth is a kind of contraction. Your energy goes inward. You do not take up space in the staff room. You do not contribute your ideas to meetings. You do not laugh as loudly. You are literally trying to take up less room because on some level you believe you do not deserve to be here. This is not about being humble. This is about being small.

---

## COMPRESSION v14
---
compression_family: C4
---
The relational consequence is that you have become someone that students want to help but do not actually respect. You are sympathetic. You are accessible. But you are not someone they aspire to be like. You have no opinions of your own. You have no confidence in your expertise. You are entirely focused on whether they think you are good. This makes you functional but not admirable. Students do not want to be like a teacher who doubts themselves.

---

## COMPRESSION v15
---
compression_family: C5
---
The recursive pattern is that low self-worth causes you to make choices that reinforce low self-worth. You stay in situations that diminish you. You accept criticism that is unfair. You accept pay that is too low. You do not advocate for yourself. Each choice is another piece of evidence that you are worthless. You are building a case against yourself. The case is compelling because the evidence is real. You created it.

---

## COMPRESSION v16
---
compression_family: C1
---
What low self-worth does to your thinking is that it makes you interpret ambiguity as rejection. A student does not participate and you assume they think you are boring. A parent does not respond to your email immediately and you assume they do not respect you. An administrator does not mention your work and you assume they think you are doing it poorly. You are reading condemnation into every interaction. You are not receiving what is actually happening. You are interpreting it through the lens of your own self-doubt.

---

## COMPRESSION v17
---
compression_family: C2
---
The price is paid in the sacrifice of your actual voice. You do not speak up in faculty meetings because you think your opinion is not valuable. You do not advocate for your students because you think you do not deserve resources. You do not end relationships that are harmful because you think you should be grateful for being chosen. You have made yourself voiceless. And silence deepens the belief that you have nothing valuable to contribute.

---

## COMPRESSION v18
---
compression_family: C3
---
Your nervous system is in a state of perpetual waiting for confirmation. Am I good? It is asking. The answer comes from outside and is always contingent. You are not good independent of external validation. So you are never good for long. You are always waiting for the next hit of approval. The nervous system is like an addict waiting for the next dose. The self-worth problem is not a thought problem. It is a nervous system problem.

---

## COMPRESSION v19
---
compression_family: C4
---
The relational impact is that you cannot celebrate another teacher's success without also comparing yourself and coming up short. A colleague gets a good evaluation and you immediately think about how you do not get one. A colleague's class achieves great results and you think about how your class is underperforming. You cannot be genuinely happy for others because their success highlights your perceived failure. Your relationships have become competitive. You have made yourself incapable of joy in what is good for others.

---

## COMPRESSION v20
---
compression_family: C5
---
The persistent pattern is that you are waiting for permission to feel valuable as a teacher. You are waiting for the administrator to tell you that you are doing well. You are waiting for the test scores to prove that you matter. You are waiting for the student evaluations to show that you are making a difference. You are waiting for someone to give you permission to feel proud. But the permission never comes from outside. So you keep waiting.

## COMPRESSION v21
---
compression_family: C1
---
The mechanism is that you have learned to derive your sense of self from external metrics instead of building it from the internal experience of your work. This means you are always seeking. You are always reaching. You are never satisfied. Someone else is always doing it better and you are always falling short. You have made yourself into a perpetual loser because you have made the game unwinnable. The only way to win is to stop playing with external scorecards.

## COMPRESSION v22
---
compression_family: C2
---
The cost is the teacher you might have become if you had believed in yourself. You might have taken risks with curriculum. You might have been more authentic with students. You might have led. You might have created something innovative. Instead, you played it safe. You protected yourself. You stayed small. Now you are paying the cost not of failure but of never actually trying. That might be the deepest cost.

## COMPRESSION v23
---
compression_family: C3
---
Your body is organized around shame. Your chest collapses slightly. Your eyes do not meet when you are in conversation. Your energy is downturned. This is not biology. This is learned behavior. Your body has learned that you are not allowed to take up space. Your body has learned that you should apologize for your teaching choices. Now your body is voting on these lessons. It is carrying the shame in how it holds itself.

## COMPRESSION v24
---
compression_family: C4
---
The relational truth is that you are trying to extract your worth from students and parents in a way that is ultimately impossible. You are asking them to carry something they cannot carry. You are asking them to convince you that you are valuable when you do not believe it yourself. The effort to convince you is exhausting for them. Eventually they give up. Then you have confirmation that you are worthless. The arrangement was never going to work.

## COMPRESSION v25
---
compression_family: C5
---
The cumulative effect is that you have built a career that is organized around proving your worth instead of living from your worth. The proving never stops. The evidence is never conclusive. The doubt never fully resolves. You are running a marathon where the finish line moves. You will exhaust yourself. You will break yourself. And you will never feel the thing you were running toward. The race is the problem.

## COMPRESSION v26
---
compression_family: C1
---
What low self-worth actually is in the deepest sense is a refusal to accept yourself as you are. It is the belief that who you are as a teacher is fundamentally inadequate. So you are trying to become someone else. You are trying to remake yourself into a teacher who is worthy. But the teacher you are trying to become is running away from the teacher you are. The chase is infinite. You will never catch yourself because you are running from yourself.

---

## COMPRESSION v27
---
compression_family: C2
---
The hidden cost is the impact on your students. When you do not believe in yourself, students sense it. They do not believe in you either. You cannot inspire them to believe in themselves when you are struggling to believe in yourself. You are modeling self-doubt. You are modeling the very thing you want them not to do. Your low self-worth is a contagion that spreads through the classroom.

## COMPRESSION v28
---
compression_family: C3
---
Your nervous system reads your self-talk as instruction. If you tell it that you are a bad teacher, it believes you. It searches for evidence. It finds plenty because confirmation bias is real. Your nervous system is not objective. It is listening to what you tell it about yourself. If what you tell it is that you are worthless, it will organize itself around proving that true. You have become your own worst enemy. Your nervous system is faithfully executing the orders you gave it.

## COMPRESSION v29
---
compression_family: C4
---
The social consequence is that you have become invisible to people who respect self-respect. You are visible to people who need to feel superior to you. You are visible to people who want to rescue you. But you are invisible to people who respect boundaries and integrity. You have organized your professional life around being visible to the wrong people. And you are not visible to the people whose respect would actually matter.

## COMPRESSION v30
---
compression_family: C5
---
The persistent pattern is that you are living downstream from a belief that you should not be a teacher. Everything you do is an apology for the space you are taking up. You are trying to earn your place in the profession by being useful, by being impressive, by being whatever you think is required. But you were already allowed to be here. You earned the degree. You earned the certification. The permission was granted. You just have not accepted it.
