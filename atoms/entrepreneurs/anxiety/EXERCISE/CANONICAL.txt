# entrepreneurs × anxiety × EXERCISE
# Slot type: EXERCISE
# Count: 30
# Voice: second-person imperative, somatic, guided
# Spec: 100–300 words, ≤10 words per instruction, explicit counts, no options, one step per sentence
# Persona grounding: desk, laptop, phone, coffee, car, journal

## EXERCISE v01
Sit at your desk. Close the laptop completely.
Place your coffee cup to your right.
Feel the weight of the cup in your hand.
Count five breaths before opening the laptop again.
Open only your email client, not the analytics.
Read the inbox from the bottom up, oldest first.
This reverses your anxiety's timeline into history.
Answer only the emails older than three days.
New emails can wait. Let them sit unread.
Type one sentence: "I am not responsible for speed."
Print this. Pin it to your monitor.
Close email. Don't check it for two hours.
When two hours pass, check email again.
Count another five breaths before you do.
Notice what didn't break in those two hours.
Nothing broke. Say this out loud three times.
Your business survived your not-checking. It will survive again.
Tomorrow, increase the time to three hours.
The anxiety will ease when you prove it wrong.

## EXERCISE v02
You're in your car between client calls. Turn off the phone.
Put the phone in the trunk, not the seat.
This physical distance creates psychological distance from it.
Sit in silence for ten minutes. Set a timer.
Let your thoughts arrive and depart like clouds.
Don't chase them. Don't fix them. Just watch them.
When the ten minutes end, retrieve the phone.
Turn it back on. Count to sixty while it boots.
During the sixty count, breathe in on "one" to "thirty."
Breathe out on "thirty-one" to "sixty."
This breath pattern activates your parasympathetic nervous system.
Check your messages. Notice you're still alive.
All the waiting didn't create catastrophe. Write that down.
Keep this note in your car for tomorrow.

## EXERCISE v03
Open your journal right now. You're sitting somewhere.
Write three things you cannot control this hour.
Write three things you actually can control this hour.
Read the controllable things out loud. Twice.
Pick one controllable thing. Do it for five minutes.
When five minutes end, stop immediately.
This proves you can contain work into time.
Time is not infinite. Your work is not infinite.
Make a list of the things you don't need to do.
Reading this list gives you permission to not do them.
Write: "I release the things I cannot control."
Say this while touching your chest. Feel the exhale.
Close the journal. Walk away from it.
Your anxiety is waiting for you to engage it again.
Don't engage it. Go work on something that matters.

## EXERCISE v05
Sit at your desk. Pull out a piece of paper.
Write the number one through ten down the left side.
Next to number one, write the worst possible outcome.
Next to two, write what you'd actually do if that happened.
Keep going through ten. Finish the list.
Read number ten back to yourself.
You could survive that. You would figure it out.
Now cross off numbers nine through one.
Because if you can survive ten, the others don't matter.
This reframe moves you from fear to capability.
Write: "I am more capable than my anxiety."
Keep this paper where you can see it.
Your brain will start to believe it if you read it.
When anxiety arrives tomorrow, read the paper again.

## EXERCISE v09
Pull out your journal. Write this sentence: "I worry because..."
Finish the sentence five different ways.
You might write: "I worry because I want to do good work."
You might write: "I worry because people depend on me."
Now, flip each worry into an affirmation.
"I want to do good work" becomes "I am doing good work."
"People depend on me" becomes "People trust me."
Write each affirmation next to its worry.
Read the affirmations aloud three times.
When you read affirmations aloud, your body hears them.
Your nervous system starts to believe them.
Now you have five affirmations to carry with you.
Post them somewhere visible.
When anxiety arrives, read them aloud again.

## EXERCISE v10
Sit somewhere quiet. Close your eyes. Time yourself for one minute.
During this minute, count each thought that arrives.
Don't stop the thoughts. Just count them.
Write down the number when the minute ends.
Now do this again, but this time breathe slowly.
Count on the inhale. Count on the exhale.
Time yourself for another minute.
Usually the second number is lower.
Slow breathing interrupts the anxiety thought pattern.
This proves you have a tool to slow the spiral.
You're not powerless. You're not helpless.
You have your breath. It's always available.
Practice this once a day for one minute.
Your anxiety will begin to fear your calmness.
