# entrepreneurs × boundaries × EXERCISE
# Slot type: EXERCISE
# Voice: second-person imperative, somatic, guided
# Spec: 100–300 words, ≤10 words per instruction, explicit counts, no options, one step per sentence

## EXERCISE v01
Open your calendar right now. Look at your week.
Find the time block with the most meetings.
Delete one meeting from that block. Just one.
The deletion is not negotiable. It's already done.
Text the person you canceled on. Keep it simple.
"I need to adjust my schedule this week. Thanks for understanding."
Don't explain. Don't apologize excessively. Don't offer alternatives yet.
This is your first boundary. It's small and it matters.
Feel the discomfort of the boundary. That's normal.
Boundaries create discomfort in the boundary-maker first.
Now sit with the opened time on your calendar.
What would you like to do with it? Write it down.
This time is yours. Not for work. For you.
Protect it like you protect client time.
Because your time is equally important.

## EXERCISE v02
Go to your phone settings. Find "Do Not Disturb."
Set it to activate at 6 PM every weekday.
Set it to deactivate at 8 AM every weekday.
This creates a boundary between your work and your life.
During Do Not Disturb, only your family can contact you.
Everyone else gets sent to voicemail.
The world will not burn down. Set it now.
In the morning, you'll see seventeen messages waiting.
They waited. They can keep waiting.
You spent one evening as a human, not a business.
Tomorrow, keep the boundary.
The messages will still be there. They always are.
Your presence on the evening was worth the waiting messages.
By Friday, you'll have reclaimed five evenings.
Five evenings is five proof points that you can have boundaries.

## EXERCISE v03
Write down your three biggest work boundaries.
Maybe it's: "I don't work before 8 AM."
Maybe it's: "I don't respond to Slack after 5 PM."
Maybe it's: "I don't take meetings on Fridays."
Write each boundary down. Print it. Put it on your desk.
Now, break the boundary once this week.
Wait. Yes, break it intentionally. With full awareness.
When you break it, write down what happened.
Did anything actually go wrong?
Usually the answer is no.
But you'll get evidence that the fear is bigger than the reality.
Next week, enforce the boundary without breaking it.
You have proof that you survive the consequences.
Proof is powerful. Proof is how boundaries become real.

## EXERCISE v04
Turn on your voicemail greeting. Write it down first.
"Hi, I'm not available right now. I check messages at 5 PM daily. Leave a message."
Record this. Listen to it. Is it clear?
Now, only check voicemail at 5 PM.
Not 4:59 PM. Not 5:01 PM. At 5 PM.
This creates predictability. Predictability is a boundary.
People will learn that you're reliable within your boundary.
By the end of the week, people will stop calling at 2 PM.
They'll know you'll call them at 5 PM.
Consistency is what makes boundaries respected.
After one week of this, add email to the boundary.
You check email at 9 AM and 5 PM only.
These are your communication windows. Everything else is work time.
Protect the work time fiercely.
Because work time is when your actual work happens.

## EXERCISE v05
Write down one project you're doing that you hate.
Write down one client relationship that drains you.
Write down one meeting you attend but don't need.
Now, create a plan to end or exit each one.
Not immediately. But with a timeline.
Maybe the project ends in three months.
Maybe the client relationship transitions to someone else.
Maybe you stop attending that meeting starting next week.
Write the timeline down. Show it to someone you trust.
Accountability makes boundaries real.
Now, tell the people involved. Do it today.
"I'm going to transition off this project by month end."
You don't need their permission. You're informing them.
Watch what happens. Usually people adapt.
Sometimes they push back. You hold the boundary anyway.
By month-end, you've reclaimed the energy this was taking.

## EXERCISE v06
Your phone is on your desk. Don't look at it for two hours.
Put it face down. Turn off the sound.
If you reach for it, put your hands behind your back.
You're training yourself to not be available all the time.
This is harder than you think. That's the point.
After two hours, you can look at your phone.
Everything is still fine. No catastrophes. No emergencies.
The world survived your two hours of unavailability.
Tomorrow, do it again but make it three hours.
By next week, you'll have a five-hour block without your phone.
This is the boundary between you and digital everything.
Notice how your mind gets quieter.
Notice how your shoulders relax without the hum of notification.
This is what boundaries feel like inside your body.

## EXERCISE v07
Pick one thing you always say yes to.
Maybe it's weekend email. Maybe it's unpaid extras.
Maybe it's taking meetings on vacation.
Now, write a script for saying no.
"I appreciate you thinking of me. I'm not available for this."
Or: "That doesn't fit in my capacity right now."
Or: "I've decided I don't work on weekends."
Write it down. Memorize it. Practice saying it out loud.
Say it three times while looking in the mirror.
You need to hear your own voice enforcing your boundary.
Next time the request comes, use your script.
You'll feel guilty. This is normal. Say the script anyway.
The guilt is your old pattern trying to stay alive.
Don't feed the old pattern. Feed the new boundary instead.
By next month, saying no will feel normal.

## EXERCISE v08
Create an auto-responder on your email.
Write: "I check email at 9 AM and 4 PM. I'll respond within 24 hours."
This sets a boundary on your response time.
It also trains people's expectations.
They'll learn you're reliable, not immediate.
Reliability is better than immediacy.
Your mental health is better than immediate response time.
Now, only check email at 9 AM and 4 PM.
Honor the boundary you've announced.
By week two, you'll notice you're less reactive.
Reactive work is usually less important anyway.
Most emails can wait. The urgent ones will escalate.
You'll have trained your nervous system to have rhythm.
Rhythm is what all humans need to stay sane.
Your business doesn't need you available every moment.
Your business needs you sane, focused, and boundaried.

## EXERCISE v09
Look at your calendar for next month.
Count the number of meetings you're in.
Now cut that number in half.
For each meeting, ask: "Do I actually need to be here?"
If the answer is no, remove yourself.
Send a message: "I don't think I add value to this meeting. I'm stepping off."
This is hard. This is necessary.
By removing yourself from unnecessary meetings, you create space.
Space is where real work happens.
Space is where thinking happens.
Most entrepreneurs are meeting-trapped.
You're going to untrap yourself.
By month-end, you'll have ten hours of your life back.
Those ten hours are sacred. Don't fill them with more meetings.
Use them for what actually matters to you.

## EXERCISE v10
Write a goodbye letter to your most demanding client.
You're not sending it. You're just writing it.
"I've decided I need to step back from working together."
Write the whole thing. Everything you'd like to say.
Now, don't send it. Read it once.
This exercise helps you separate from the fantasy that you must stay.
You could leave if you needed to.
Just knowing this is leaving power.
Now put the letter away. Don't send it.
Instead, with this newfound clarity, set one boundary with this client.
Maybe it's: "I'm not available for calls before 10 AM."
Maybe it's: "Projects take a minimum of two weeks."
Enforce this boundary with full confidence.
You know you could leave. So you don't have to.
This paradox is where real power lives.

## EXERCISE v12
---
Sit down in a chair with your feet on the ground.
Turn your phone completely off. Not silent. Off.
Set a timer for exactly five minutes.
Close your eyes and breathe slowly.
Count to four on the inhale.
Hold for four counts.
Count to four on the exhale.
Repeat this cycle for the full five minutes.
Don't meditate. Don't fix anything.
Just breathe.
When the timer sounds, open your eyes.
Notice that nothing terrible happened in five minutes.
Your work is still there. The crisis is not.
Turn your phone back on.
You are now five minutes less anxious.
