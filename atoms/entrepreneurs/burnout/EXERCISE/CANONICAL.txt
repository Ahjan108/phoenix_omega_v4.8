# entrepreneurs × burnout × building the thing that's building you
# Slot type: EXERCISE
# Count: 30

---
## EXERCISE v01
Separate yourself from the business. Step 1: Write down where you end and the business begins. Step 2: Usually the boundaries are completely blurred. Step 3: That fusion is the problem. The business will survive if you step back. Try it.

---
## EXERCISE v02
Define what success looks like when you're done. Step 1: What outcome would let you rest? Step 2: Be specific: revenue number, user count, timeline. Step 3: If you can't define it, you're chasing infinite and you'll never rest.

---
## EXERCISE v03
Audit your support system. Step 1: Who do you actually talk to about how hard this is? Step 2: If the answer is no one, that's the problem. Step 3: The isolation is part of what's killing you. Build real support.

---
## EXERCISE v04
Calculate what this is costing. Step 1: What have you sacrificed? Relationships, health, joy, sleep, time. Step 2: Put a value on it. Step 3: Is what you're building worth what you're trading? Be honest.

---
## EXERCISE v05
Notice the pressure you're putting on yourself. Step 1: What are you avoiding through busyness? Fear? Uncertainty? Step 2: That avoidance is expensive. Step 3: What would happen if you let yourself feel it?

---
## EXERCISE v06
Set a boundary that actually matters. Step 1: Pick something sacred: weekends, evenings, one day a week. Step 2: Actually protect it. Step 3: The world won't collapse. The business will find a way. You need to find a way too.

---
## EXERCISE v07
Identify what you're afraid would happen if you stepped back. Step 1: What's the catastrophe you're preventing by being on all the time? Step 2: How likely is it actually? Step 3: Usually the fear is 95 percent imagination, 5 percent real.

---
## EXERCISE v08
Talk to someone who sold their company or stepped back. Step 1: What did they learn? Step 2: What would they do differently? Step 3: Their experience might illuminate what you're not willing to see.

---
## EXERCISE v09
Write what burnt-out you looks like. Step 1: Be specific about the signs. Step 2: Are you already there? Step 3: If yes, you're in crisis mode. That's important to acknowledge.

---
## EXERCISE v10
Name one thing you could delegate that you're not. Step 1: What's taking your time that someone else could handle? Step 2: Why aren't you delegating it? Step 3: Usually: control, fear, lack of trust. Work with that.

---
## EXERCISE v11
Track where your energy goes. Step 1: For one week, note what drains you vs. what replenishes you. Step 2: Compare the ratio. Step 3: If it's 95 drain / 5 replenish, that's the problem.

---
## EXERCISE v12
Ask your team how you're doing. Step 1: Not about the business. About you. Step 2: They probably see the burnout more clearly than you do. Step 3: Hearing it from them might land differently than hearing it from yourself.

---
## EXERCISE v13
Define the minimum viable company. Step 1: What does the business need from you right now to survive? Step 2: What else are you doing beyond that? Step 3: Usually there's significant extra. That's where relief lives.

---
## EXERCISE v14
Create a real contingency plan. Step 1: What if you became unavailable tomorrow? Step 2: Would the business survive? Step 3: If no, you've built something fragile that depends entirely on you. That's the problem.

---
## EXERCISE v15
Notice when you're running on adrenaline vs. passion. Step 1: Are you excited or terrified? Step 2: Usually burnt-out founders are running on terror. Step 3: Terror is not sustainable fuel.

---
## EXERCISE v16
Do one thing that's not about the business. Step 1: Actually do it. Not think about it. Do it. Step 2: Notice the guilt or anxiety. Step 3: That reaction shows you how colonized your life has become.

---
## EXERCISE v17
Ask: am I making decisions from inspiration or desperation? Step 1: Look at recent major decisions. Step 2: Which motive drove them? Step 3: Desperation decisions usually end up in regret.

---
## EXERCISE v18
Calculate your runway. Step 1: How long until you have to make a decision (about money, about stepping back, about direction)? Step 2: Give yourself a timeline. Step 3: Working toward that timeline is different than infinite stress.

---
## EXERCISE v19
Name what you're no longer capable of doing. Step 1: The burnout is probably affecting your actual decision-making. Step 2: What decisions shouldn't you be making right now? Step 3: It's okay to admit you're too burnt out for certain calls.

---
## EXERCISE v20
Identify your exit fantasy. Step 1: What would you do if you didn't have to run the business? Step 2: Why aren't you doing it? Step 3: Sometimes the answer is: the business is actually the problem, not the solution.

---
## EXERCISE v21
Notice the stories you're telling yourself. Step 1: "If I don't do this, ___." "If I rest, ___." Step 2: Are these true or are they the fear talking? Step 3: Most of them are fear. See them as fear, not fact.

---
## EXERCISE v22
Create a health metric for yourself. Step 1: Just like you track business metrics, track a personal metric (sleep, exercise, joy). Step 2: If it's declining, that's as important as any revenue decline. Step 3: You don't optimize your health to keep the business running. The business should support your health.

---
## EXERCISE v23
Ask what kind of founder you're becoming. Step 1: Who do you want to be known as? Step 2: Who are you actually becoming? Step 3: The gap is worth looking at.

---
## EXERCISE v24
Do the investor pitch for your own well-being. Step 1: Make the case for why you need to change something. Step 2: What's the ROI of a healthier, more present founder? Step 3: If you can make that pitch to investors, make it to yourself.

---
## EXERCISE v25
Identify the moment it shifted. Step 1: When did passion become obligation? Step 2: When did excitement become fear? Step 3: That moment is important. It shows you when the trajectory changed.

---
## EXERCISE v26
Notice when you're hiding behind business metrics. Step 1: Revenue is good, users are growing—but how are you? Step 2: The business can look great while you're falling apart. Step 3: The second matters more.

---
## EXERCISE v27
Set a hard stop time once a week. Step 1: Pick one day. Step 2: Work ends at a specific time. No exceptions. Step 3: After 12 weeks, assess. Has the business suffered? Usually not.

---
## EXERCISE v28
Write what life could look like in five years if you keep this pace. Step 1: Be honest. Health gone? Relationships destroyed? Perspective lost? Step 2: Is that the future you're building toward? Step 3: You can change direction.

---
## EXERCISE v29
Ask for help. Step 1: Not with the business. With yourself. Step 2: A therapist, a coach, a friend. Someone outside the system. Step 3: You've been carrying this alone. You don't have to.

---
## EXERCISE v30
Do the math on whether this is worth it. Step 1: What are you earning in money? Step 2: What are you paying in health, time, relationships? Step 3: Is the exchange fair? Usually at a certain point, it's not.
