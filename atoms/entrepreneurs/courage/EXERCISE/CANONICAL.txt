# entrepreneurs × courage × EXERCISE
# Slot type: EXERCISE
# Voice: second-person imperative, somatic, guided
# Spec: 100–300 words, ≤10 words per instruction, explicit counts, no options, one step per sentence

---
## EXERCISE v01
Write down the decision you've been avoiding.
Maybe it's raising prices. Maybe it's firing someone.
Maybe it's pivoting the whole company.
Write it down. One sentence. That's the decision.
Now write the fear next to it.
What are you afraid will happen?
Usually the fear is worse than the reality.
Tomorrow, you're going to do the thing.
Not think about it. Not prepare for it.
Just do it. With whatever courage you have.
You don't need to feel brave to be brave.
Brave is action even when you're shaking.
Brave is doing the thing at 8 AM tomorrow.
Set an alarm. Do it early. Before your fear wakes up.
By 8:30 AM, it will be done.
By 9 AM, you'll realize you survived.
That survival is what courage actually feels like.

## EXERCISE v02
Stand up. Put your hand on your chest.
Feel your heartbeat while you say this:
"I am brave enough to make hard choices."
Say it five times. Feel how it lands in your body.
Now step forward with your left foot. Just one step.
This physical movement is metaphor.
You're moving forward even when you're afraid.
Hold that foot forward for five seconds.
Your body is already braver than your mind.
Your body knows how to do hard things.
Trust your body more than your anxious mind.
Tomorrow, when you need courage, remember this step.
Your body will lead where your mind can't.
Courage is physical. It starts in your feet.
Practice this step every morning for a week.
By week's end, courage becomes your posture.

## EXERCISE v03
Write a permission slip to yourself.

Now tear out each permission slip.
Carry one with you. Put it in your pocket.
When courage falters, pull it out and read it.
You've given yourself the permission you were waiting for.
No one else gets to give you permission.
You're the only authority you actually need.
By the end of the week, you'll have implemented all five.
Permission is the gateway to action.

## EXERCISE v05
Get a piece of paper. Write your fear on it.
"I'm afraid people will leave."
"I'm afraid I'll lose money."
"I'm afraid I'll regret this."
Look at the fear. Feel how true it seems.
Now, sit with it for two minutes.
During those two minutes, do nothing. Just sit with it.
The fear won't kill you. It's just a thought.
After two minutes, do the brave thing anyway.
Do it scared. Do it uncertain.
Courage isn't the absence of fear.
Courage is action in spite of fear.
You've got this backward. You don't need to feel brave first.
You need to do the brave thing first.
The brave-feeling comes after the brave-action.
So move. Act. Do the thing.
The fear can ride along. It doesn't have to stop you.

## EXERCISE v06
Call the person who needs to hear what you're about to say.
Maybe it's a client. Maybe it's your team.
Don't prepare a speech. Don't write it down.
Just call them and start talking.
Your natural speaking is more brave than your prepared speech.
Tell them the truth. Tell them your plan.
Tell them what's changing.
Speak for two minutes. Then stop talking.
Let them respond.
You don't have to agree with them.
You just have to be clear about what's happening.
Clarity is an act of courage.
By the end of the call, you'll feel the weight lift.
The decision is no longer hidden. It's public. It's real.
This is what courage feels like. Not fearlessness.
Just the relief of finally saying the hard thing.

## EXERCISE v12
---
Sit down in a chair with your feet on the ground.
Turn your phone completely off. Not silent. Off.
Set a timer for exactly five minutes.
Close your eyes and breathe slowly.
Count to four on the inhale.
Hold for four counts.
Count to four on the exhale.
Repeat this cycle for the full five minutes.
Don't meditate. Don't fix anything.
Just breathe.
When the timer sounds, open your eyes.
Notice that nothing terrible happened in five minutes.
Your work is still there. The crisis is not.
Turn your phone back on.
You are now five minutes less anxious.

## EXERCISE v16
---
Create an email filter for your most stressful emails.
Direct them to a specific folder labeled "3 PM."
Set a phone reminder for 3 PM and 5 PM.
Between now and 3 PM, don't check the folder.
This creates a boundary.
The boundary protects your morning focus.
You're not ignoring problems. You're batching them.
Batching problems is more effective than scattering anxiety.
At 3 PM, open the folder and respond.
You've created a system of protection.
Systems are more reliable than willpower.
Work on your system, not just your willpower.
This system works because it removes the constant decision.
The constant decision was exhausting you.
The system lets you rest.
