# entrepreneurs × grief × EXERCISE
# Slot type: EXERCISE
# Voice: second-person imperative, somatic, guided
# Spec: 100–300 words, ≤10 words per instruction, explicit counts, no options, one step per sentence

---
## EXERCISE v01
Sit down. Write a letter to your co-founder.
Not to send. Just to write.
"I miss you. I'm angry. I'm trying to keep going."
Write everything. The gratitude. The resentment. All of it.
Get it all out of your body and onto paper.
When you're done, you can read it or burn it.
Either way, you've moved the grief.
Grief that stays inside hardens into bitterness.
Grief that moves becomes bearable.
This week, write the letter.
By next month, you'll notice you're lighter.
Lighter doesn't mean the loss is gone.
It means you've made space for both loss and life.

## EXERCISE v02
Go to the place where you built things together.
The coffee shop. The office. The couch.
Sit there. Let yourself feel what you feel.
Sadness is allowed. Anger is allowed. Gratitude is allowed.
Sit for ten minutes. Just sit.
You're not trying to fix anything.
You're just witnessing what was.
When ten minutes end, thank the space.
Thank the space for being there.
Then leave. Come back next week.
By month-end, you've honored the past five times.
Honoring the past lets you move forward.

## EXERCISE v03
Write down three things your co-founder brought to the company.
Not in anger. In honesty.
"They brought belief when I had doubt."
"They pushed me to think bigger."
"They made me better at what I do."
Write three. These are true things.
Now, write three things you bring to the company.
Things only you have.
"I bring steadiness when things shift."
"I bring practical thinking."
"I bring commitment to the long game."
Write three. Read both lists.
You're not the same person anymore.
You're not the same partnership.
But you're not nothing. You're different.
Different is how you move forward.

## EXERCISE v04
Call someone you both knew. Not to be sad together.
To remember together.
"Remember that thing they said about..."
"I was just thinking about the time when..."
Share memories. Laugh if you can.
The laughter doesn't betray the loss.
Laughter honors the life that was.
By the end of the call, something will have shifted.
The person won't just be absence.
They'll be memory. That's different.
This week, call one person. Share one memory.

## EXERCISE v06

By identifying them, you honor them.
By honoring them, you move past them.
These specific things will live with you forever.
That's okay. Forever is what grief asks for.

## EXERCISE v11
---
Stand up right now, wherever you are.
Press your feet firmly into the ground.
Raise both arms overhead and stretch them.
Hold this stretch for three full breaths.
Lower your arms slowly and feel the blood flow.
Walk to a window and look outside for thirty seconds.
Find one living thing and observe it.
Return to your desk and sit.
You've been in motion for three minutes.
Notice: the world is still moving without you.
Your anxiety hasn't stopped progress.
Your work is waiting but it can wait three more minutes.
Take another intentional breath.
You've reset your nervous system.
Return to work from this reset.

## EXERCISE v12
---
Sit down in a chair with your feet on the ground.
Turn your phone completely off. Not silent. Off.
Set a timer for exactly five minutes.
Close your eyes and breathe slowly.
Count to four on the inhale.
Hold for four counts.
Count to four on the exhale.
Repeat this cycle for the full five minutes.
Don't meditate. Don't fix anything.
Just breathe.
When the timer sounds, open your eyes.
Notice that nothing terrible happened in five minutes.
Your work is still there. The crisis is not.
Turn your phone back on.
You are now five minutes less anxious.

## EXERCISE v13
---
Pull out a piece of paper right now.
Write down three things you can control today.
Write down three things you cannot control.
Read both lists out loud.
Say: I release what I cannot control.
Circle one item from the cannot-control list.
Tear that item off the paper.
Keep the three control items where you can see them.
The tearing is symbolic.
The symbol is power.
When anxiety tries to make you responsible for uncontrollables, check the list.
You've already decided: not your responsibility.
Keep the control items in your pocket.
Touch them when you need reminder.
The touching is proof of your agency.

## EXERCISE v14
---
Put on a song that makes your body want to move.
Play it through once all the way.
Move your body however it wants to move.
Dance. Sway. Jump. Shake.
Expressive movement, not exercise movement.
Don't think about it. Just move.
When the song ends, sit down.
Notice: your mind is quieter.
Anxiety lives in stillness.
Movement is the antidote.
You have access to this anytime.
A three-minute song is a three-minute break.
Add three-minute breaks to your day.
Add them between difficult tasks.
Movement is your emergency reset button.

## EXERCISE v15
---
Open a journal or blank document.
Write the absolute worst scenario you're anxious about.
Write it completely. Don't soften it.
Now write: If this happened, I would:
Write five concrete things you would actually do.
Now write: I have survived:
List five hard things you've already survived.
The hardest thing you've survived is harder than your anxiety.
Read the survival list.
Close the journal.
You don't have to think about worst cases.
You only have to be present right now.
Right now is safe.
Write one true thing: Today I showed up.
The showing up is enough.

## EXERCISE v16
---
Create an email filter for your most stressful emails.
Direct them to a specific folder labeled "3 PM."
Set a phone reminder for 3 PM and 5 PM.
Between now and 3 PM, don't check the folder.
This creates a boundary.
The boundary protects your morning focus.
You're not ignoring problems. You're batching them.
Batching problems is more effective than scattering anxiety.
At 3 PM, open the folder and respond.
You've created a system of protection.
Systems are more reliable than willpower.
Work on your system, not just your willpower.
This system works because it removes the constant decision.
The constant decision was exhausting you.
The system lets you rest.

The work is better when you're grounded.

## EXERCISE v22
---
Do a complete body scan (ten minutes).
Start at your head.
Is it tense? Consciously relax it.
Move down: face, jaw, neck, shoulders.
Continue: chest, belly, back, arms, hands.
Continue: hips, legs, feet.
Each body part, find any tension and release it.
Your body holds your anxiety.
The scan retrieves it from your body.
The releasing is how you let it go.
When you finish, notice the calm.
This calm is always available inside you.
You're just learning to access it.
Practice this daily. The calm increases.
Your nervous system begins to remember safety.

## EXERCISE v23
---
Identify the core limiting belief beneath your anxiety.
Mine: If I'm not perfect, I'll fail.
Write it. Examine it.
Is it true? Whose voice is this?
Write a counter-belief.
Mine: Good enough is enough.
Repeat the counter-belief three times.
Believe it fifty percent. That's progress.
The belief doesn't have to be complete.
It just has to be more true than the anxiety's lie.
Write the counter-belief where you can see it.
Read it every morning.
The repetition rewires your neural pathways.
The rewiring takes time. It works.
Plant the new belief. Let it grow.
