## EXERCISE v01
The 72-hour rule. Before you say yes to something, wait 72 hours.

Step 1: Someone asks you to cover a shift, to pick up extra work, to take on another responsibility. Your instinct is yes. Stop. Write down what they asked.

Step 2: Wait 72 hours. Don't decide for three days. In those three days, live with the question. What would saying yes cost you. What would saying no cost you.

Step 3: After 72 hours, decide. Most people discover the automatic yes wasn't actually yes. It was fear.

## EXERCISE v02
The boundary statement. Write it down and say it out loud.

Step 1: Finish this sentence: I am not available for...

Step 2: Your answer might be extra shifts after day six. Might be phone calls during dinner. Might be work in your home office after dark.

Step 3: Say it out loud to yourself three times. Write it in your phone notes. You're not avoiding responsibility. You're defining your responsibility limits.

---
## EXERCISE v03
The family time block. Protect at least one thing.

Step 1: Pick one hour a week. Saturday breakfast. Wednesday dinner. Sunday morning. Something that happens at the same time every week.

Step 2: That hour is not available. Tell your station. Tell your crew. Tell the person who usually calls you. This hour is blocked.

Step 3: Treat it like you'd treat a critical call. Don't cancel it. Don't reschedule it. It's protected time. Your family sees consistency. Your nervous system learns that you're present somewhere.

## EXERCISE v04
The phone boundary. Physical distance from the device.

Step 1: Pick a time of day. Could be dinner. Could be before bed. A one-hour window.

Step 2: Put your phone in another room. Not silent. Not on a table nearby. Another room. You can't reach it without walking.

Step 3: Do this for one week. Seven times. You're teaching your system that you're available on a schedule, not always.

## EXERCISE v05
The no practice. Say it to low-stakes requests.

Step 1: Tomorrow, someone will ask you for something small. Someone will ask directions or to pass something or to help with something minor.

Step 2: Practice your no on small things. No, I'm not going that way. No, I can't lift that. No, I don't have time for that right now.

Step 3: The muscle gets stronger. By practicing no on small requests, you're training yourself so that big requests don't automatically get yes.

## EXERCISE v06
The duty inventory. List what you actually have to do.

Step 1: Write down your actual responsibilities. Your job. Your family. The commitments you made in writing.

Step 2: Everything else is choice. Volunteering for extra shifts. Staying late. Taking on mentoring. These are choices, not duties.

Step 3: Look at your list. You might realize you have more choice than you thought. That choice can be wielded.

## EXERCISE v07
The transition ritual. Create a formal end to work before you're home.

Step 1: Identify a point between work and home. A street. A stop. A drive. Somewhere between station and house.

Step 2: At that point, do something deliberate. Take off your work jacket. Change the music. Say it out loud: work is over.

Step 3: Your brain needs to know that one thing has ended and another has begun. Without the transition, work follows you home.

## EXERCISE v08
The mutual aid rule. You can't help everyone every time.

Step 1: When someone asks you for something, ask: Do they have any other options.

Step 2: If the answer is yes, you're not their only option. Mutual aid means the burden is shared. You're one option, not the only option.

Step 3: If you're the only option, that's information. That's different from being the easiest option. Make sure you know the difference.

## EXERCISE v09
The crew conversation. Talk about coverage before you're asked.

Step 1: Bring it up in the station. Not in crisis. Just conversation. You're thinking about coverage and how the duty is shared.

Step 2: Ask how everyone else is handling it. You might discover others are struggling with the same thing.

Step 3: Normalize the conversation about limits. You're not abandoning the crew by setting boundaries. You're modeling how to be sustainable.

## EXERCISE v10
The availability window. Define when you're available for non-emergency requests.

Step 1: Pick your window. Maybe it's after shift hours and before midnight. Maybe it's weekends except Sunday afternoon.

Step 2: Tell people your availability. Captain. Crew. Friends. This is when I can take calls. This is when I can respond.

Step 3: Outside that window, you're not available. That's not selfish. That's sustainable.

## EXERCISE v11
The three-request rule. When three people ask you for something in a week, you pause.

Step 1: Keep track of asks. Not in paranoia. Just awareness. When three separate people ask you for something, that's your signal.

Step 2: That's when you step back. That week, you say no to additional requests. You're overloaded.

Step 3: You're not being selfish. You're being accurate about your capacity. You've hit your limit.

## EXERCISE v12
The priority ranking. Know what comes first.

Step 1: Rank your priorities. Your health. Your family. Your crew. Your job. Your extended commitments. Order them honestly.

Step 2: When you get asked to do something, check your ranking. Does this align with my top three priorities.

Step 3: If not, the answer is easier. It's not a priority for you. You can say no and mean it.

## EXERCISE v13
The energy audit. Know what actually costs you.

Step 1: Track for one week what drains your energy. Extra shifts. Phone calls. Meetings. Certain people. Write it down.

Step 2: Look at your list. Some of these things have choices. Some don't. Mark which is which.

Step 3: For the choices, you have options. For the non-choices, you have strategies. You're learning your actual constraints.

## EXERCISE v14
The family conversation. Tell your people what's changing.

Step 1: Sit down with your family. Tell them you're working on boundaries. You're going to be more present.

Step 2: Tell them specific things. You're not taking calls during dinner. You've blocked this hour for us. You're going to try to make this event.

Step 3: Name it so they know what to expect. They might be skeptical based on past promises. You're earning back credibility through actions.

## EXERCISE v15
The delegation opportunity. When someone asks, ask them to problem-solve.

Step 1: Someone comes to you with a request. Instead of solving it, ask them: What's your idea for handling this.

Step 2: They might surprise you. They might have an idea. Or they might realize it's not actually your problem.

Step 3: You're teaching people that you're not the only problem-solver. You're teaching yourself that not all problems are yours.

## EXERCISE v16
The cost accounting. What has overavailability cost you.

Step 1: Write down what you've missed because you said yes to work.

Step 2: Family events. Relationships. Your health. Sleep. Recovery. Your own healing. What's the actual list.

Step 3: Look at that list. Those are the people and things that matter. Your availability to them matters. You're rebalancing.

## EXERCISE v17
The shift schedule boundary. Mark what you will and won't do.

Step 1: Look at next month's schedule. Mark in your calendar which shifts you will work and which you won't.

Step 2: For one month, don't deviate. You made the decision consciously. You follow it.

Step 3: After one month, assess. Did the world end. Did your crew survive. Did you have more resources for home. That's data.

## EXERCISE v18
The language update. Change how you say yes.

Step 1: Instead of immediately saying yes, say: Let me check and get back to you.

Step 2: That pause creates space. It prevents the automatic response. It gives you time to consult your priorities.

Step 3: You're training yourself out of the Pavlovian yes. You're creating a moment where choice exists.

## EXERCISE v19
The off-duty protection. Your time off is actually off.

Step 1: On your days off, don't check the schedule. Don't think about coverage. Don't strategize about who's working.

Step 2: If the station calls, be honest: I'm off-duty today. Get back to me when I'm scheduled.

Step 3: They'll manage. They've managed every time you've had actual leave. You can have protected time.

---
## EXERCISE v20
The habit interrupt. Break the automatic path to the station.

Step 1: On your day off, don't go to the station. Don't drive by. Don't stop in to check on things.

Step 2: You're teaching yourself that not going in is an option. That the station functions without you being there physically.

Step 3: Do this for two weeks. You'll notice the habit pull. You'll resist it. You'll realize how deep the pattern runs.

## EXERCISE v21
The guilt work. Separate guilt from obligation.

Step 1: When you say no to something, you'll feel guilty. That's automatic. Write down what the guilt is.

Step 2: Then ask: Am I actually responsible for this, or does it just feel like I am.

Step 3: Guilt isn't evidence of obligation. It's just evidence that you care. You can care and still have boundaries.

## EXERCISE v22
The mentor conversation. Ask someone who has boundaries how they do it.

Step 1: There's someone at your station or in your department who says no. Who doesn't work every extra shift. Who leaves when their shift ends.

Step 2: Talk to them. Ask how they do it. What's the story they tell themselves. How do they handle the pressure.

Step 3: They might give you permission. Permission to be different. Permission to be sustainable.

## EXERCISE v23
The text boundary. Don't text or call during protected hours.

Step 1: Pick times you won't initiate contact about work. Off your shift times. Family time. Sleep time.

Step 2: If you have urgent work thoughts, write them down. Handle them during work hours.

Step 3: You're teaching people that you're boundaried. That work has parameters. That you're not available always.

## EXERCISE v24
The favor accounting. Know what you've given without being asked to give back.

Step 1: Make a list of favors you've done. Shifts covered. Calls taken. Help given. Extra effort expended.

Step 2: Look at that list. Did people ask you to go into debt. Or did you volunteer all of it.

Step 3: You have leverage. You've done work that was asked and work that wasn't. You can choose to do less of the unasked work.

## EXERCISE v25
The station feedback loop. Tell them your boundaries.

Step 1: Tell the captain. Tell the chief. Tell your crew. I'm working on being more sustainable. This means I won't be available for all extra shifts.

Step 2: This is not abandonment. This is honesty. This is saying you matter too.

Step 3: Some people will respect this. Some won't. You're finding out who values sustainability and who just values extraction.

## EXERCISE v26
The conversation script. Practice what you'll say when asked.

Step 1: Write down what someone might ask and what you'll say. Can you cover Tuesday. I'm not available Tuesday.

Step 2: Practice saying it. Out loud. Without apology. Without explanation.

Step 3: The fewer words you use, the stronger the boundary. No is a complete sentence. I'm not available is a complete sentence.

## EXERCISE v27
The family reclamation. Do one thing you stopped doing.

Step 1: Identify something you used to do with family that you've stopped doing. Game night. Cooking together. Watching something.

Step 2: Schedule it. Mark it in the calendar like you'd mark a shift.

Step 3: Do it. You're claiming back parts of your life. You're showing your family that you're reorienting toward them.

## EXERCISE v28
The energy protection. Say no to things that don't energize you.

Step 1: What are you saying yes to that depletes you. Certain people. Certain responsibilities. Certain types of work.

Step 2: For the next month, say no to at least one of those things once per week.

Step 3: Your energy is a resource. You're allowed to protect it. You're allowed to use it on things that matter.

## EXERCISE v29
The perspective flip. What would you tell someone else.

Step 1: A crew member comes to you exhausted from extra shifts. They've lost time with family. They're running on fumes.

Step 2: What would you tell them. You'd say they need boundaries. You'd say they matter. You'd say they're not responsible for everyone's coverage.

Step 3: Say those things to yourself now. You deserve the advice you give others.

---
## EXERCISE v30
The sustainable model. Commit to one boundary for 30 days.

Step 1: Pick one boundary. One thing you'll do differently. Leave at shift end. Don't answer work calls after nine PM. Block one family night.

Step 2: Do it for 30 days. The crew will adjust. Your family will adjust. You will adjust.

Step 3: After 30 days, assess. How did it feel. What changed. Then decide to keep going or adjust.
