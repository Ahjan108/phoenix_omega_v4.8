path: The night the dispatch didn't stop
BAND: 1
SEMANTIC_FAMILY: SF1
---
Back to back. That was the shift. Seven hours of dispatch. Every time you cleared a scene there was another one queued up. Another address. Another emergency. Your nervous system was running hot but your training kicked in. You move. You respond. You don't think about how tired your body is because the next call is already happening. By hour six your mind was doing the work on fumes. Your hands still knew the protocols. Your crew still moved with precision. But something inside you was reaching for a bottom that wouldn't come.

BAND: 2
SEMANTIC_FAMILY: SF3
---
Most calls have an ending. You treat it or you don't. You transport or you stabilize. You fight the fire or you ventilate. There's closure in the protocol. But that call didn't have one. The dispatch sent you to a situation that nobody had trained for. The victim was alive but not responsive. The family was requesting something that wasn't in the protocols. Your crew looked at each other because the usual logic didn't apply. You did what you could and left the scene without an ending. The overwhelm came from the incompleteness of it.

The day off is supposed to be a reset. Twenty four hours to downregulate. But the shift before the day off is different. Your body knows it's coming and something in your nervous system tries to cram as much as it can into those last hours. Every call feels heavier. Every scene feels like the last one before freedom. And then the calls back up. Four pages in two hours. Your mind is already at home and your body is still responding. The overwhelm is in the contradiction. You're mentally checked out but physically still holding the weight.

Three shifts back to back. That's the scheduling sometimes. Your body runs on an internal timer that's designed for recovery. Forty eight hours off. But sometimes you're back in forty six.
