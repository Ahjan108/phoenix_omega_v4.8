# first_responders × courage × the kind of courage they don't talk about at briefings
# Slot type: EXERCISE
# Count: 30

---
## EXERCISE v01
The one sentence practice. Start very small.

Step 1: Identify one person you trust. Not your whole crew. One person.

Step 2: Write down one sentence about what the job has cost you. One thing. Not a whole story. One sentence.

Step 3: Say that sentence out loud to that person. That's courage. Small courage. Real courage. You're breaking the silence.

---
## EXERCISE v02
The debrief conversation. After a hard call, actually talk about the impact.

Step 1: Within 24 hours of a difficult call, sit down with someone you trust.

Step 2: Don't talk about what happened. Talk about how it affected you. One thing.

Step 3: You're normalizing the conversation about impact. You're modeling that talking about it is okay.

---
## EXERCISE v03
The phone call practice. Name what you need.

Step 1: Identify what you actually need. Support. Professional help. Just someone to listen.

Step 2: Make the call. To your therapist. To your partner. To your mentor. One call.

Step 3: You're teaching yourself that asking is possible. That saying I need something isn't weakness.

---
## EXERCISE v04
The honesty check. Tell the truth once.

Step 1: The next time someone asks how you are, tell the truth. Not all of it. But the real answer.

Step 2: You're not fine. You're struggling. You're okay but not fine. Pick the true one.

Step 3: Notice what happens when you tell the truth. Most people can handle it. The fear is usually bigger than the reality.

---
## EXERCISE v05
The mirror conversation. Talk to yourself about the change.

Step 1: Stand in the mirror. Look at yourself. Say one true thing about how the job has changed you.

Step 2: Don't try to fix it. Just acknowledge it. Acknowledge happens before change.

Step 3: Repeat this once a week. You're getting used to the change being real. You're not denying it anymore.

---
## EXERCISE v06
The written truth. Write what you won't say.

Step 1: Write down what you haven't told anyone. The impact. The cost. The struggle. Write it all.

Step 2: You don't have to share it. Write it for yourself. Get it out of your head.

Step 3: The act of writing makes it real. Reality is the foundation for courage.

---
## EXERCISE v07
The small decision. Choose vulnerability once.

Step 1: Identify one small way you could be vulnerable about the job.

Step 2: Could be telling someone a story. Could be admitting you're afraid. Could be asking for help with something small.

Step 3: Do the one small vulnerable thing. You're building the courage muscle on low stakes first.

---
## EXERCISE v08
The crew conversation. Normalize the harder stuff.

Step 1: In the station, bring up something that's hard. Not in crisis. Just conversation.

Step 2: Say one true thing about the job being harder than it was before. You're giving others permission to be honest.

Step 3: You might discover others have been waiting for someone to go first. Breaking silence spreads.

---
## EXERCISE v09
The reflection practice. What courage have you already shown.

Step 1: Write down three times you've been brave. Doesn't have to be dramatic. Could be small brave.

Step 2: Read the list. You've already done this. You already have courage.

Step 3: You're building evidence that courage is possible. You've done it before.

## EXERCISE v10
The appointment scheduling. Take one concrete step toward help.

Step 1: Schedule an appointment. Therapist. Counselor. Employee assistance program. Whatever applies.

Step 2: Schedule it this week. Make it real. Put it in your calendar.

Step 3: Scheduling is 80 percent of the courage. Showing up is the other 20 percent. Start with the scheduling.

## EXERCISE v11
The mentor reach out. Find someone who's already done the hard thing.

Step 1: Identify someone you respect who's been honest about the job changing them.

Step 2: Send them a message. Ask if they'd be willing to talk. One conversation.

Step 3: You're learning from someone who's already walked this path. You're not alone.

## EXERCISE v12
The family conversation. Tell the people who matter.

Step 1: Sit down with your partner or your family. Tell them one true thing about how the job has impacted you.

Step 2: You don't have to fix it right now. You just have to say it.

Step 3: The people who love you already suspect. Being honest is freeing for them too.

## EXERCISE v13
The gratitude toward your own bravery. Notice when you're being courageous.

Step 1: This week, do one brave thing. Ask for help. Tell the truth. Set a boundary.

Step 2: After you do it, acknowledge it. You did that. That was brave.

Step 3: You're rewiring your brain to recognize courage when it happens. It's already happening.

## EXERCISE v14
The body check. How does courage feel physically.

Step 1: Next time you do something brave, notice what's happening in your body.

Step 2: Shaking. Heart racing. Stomach tight. That's not weakness. That's courage being expressed physically.

Step 3: You're learning that courage doesn't feel comfortable. Courage feels like fear. Do it anyway.

## EXERCISE v15
The small group sharing. Test vulnerability with a few.

Step 1: Find two or three people you trust. Could be crew. Could be friends outside work.

Step 2: Tell them you've been struggling. Not the whole story. Just that you've been struggling.

Step 3: You're testing the waters. You're building evidence that people can handle honesty.

## EXERCISE v16
The peer support connection. Know where the resources are.

Step 1: Find out about peer support in your department or nearby.

Step 2: Just get the information. No commitment. Just know it exists.

Step 3: Having the information is having options. Options equal power. Power equals courage.

## EXERCISE v17
The one conversation after a hard call. Don't process alone.

Step 1: After the next really difficult call, find one person within 12 hours.

Step 2: Tell them one thing. What stayed with you. What you're worried about. One true thing.

Step 3: You're breaking the pattern of processing alone. You're building new neural pathways toward connection.

## EXERCISE v18
The shame inventory. Name what you're afraid to admit.

Step 1: What are you most ashamed to admit about the job's impact on you.

Step 2: Write it down. Name the shame. Naming it takes power away from it.

Step 3: Shame thrives in silence. Speaking it is the antidote. Just the naming is the start.

## EXERCISE v19
The permission practice. Give yourself permission to not be fine.

Step 1: Say it out loud. I give myself permission to not be fine.

Step 2: Say it again. I don't have to be okay. I can be struggling.

Step 3: You're rescinding the permission that the culture gave you to stay silent. You're writing new rules for yourself.

## EXERCISE v20
The small admission. Admit something tiny.

Step 1: Something small that's been hard. The job is changing how you see things. You're scared sometimes.

Step 2: Tell one person. Not the crew. Someone outside.

Step 3: Small admissions are practice for bigger ones. You're building the skill of being honest.

The call that changed you conversation. Name the specific call.

Step 1: Think of one call that changed how you see the world. Name it.

Step 2: Tell someone about that call. Not clinical details. What it did to your thinking.

Step 3: Naming the call that changed you is powerful. It acknowledges the impact was real.

Step 1: Write down what you were taught strength looks like. Unshaken. Self-sufficient. Handling alone.

Step 2: Write down what you think strength looks like now. Could include vulnerability. Could include asking for help.

Step 3: You're redefining strength for yourself. Strength is also knowing when to ask for help.

Step 1: Identify someone who's further along in processing the job's impact.

Step 3: You're collecting evidence that this path is possible. You're not blazing the trail alone.

## EXERCISE v24
The incremental courage. Do one brave thing per week.

Step 1: This week, do one brave thing. Small. Could be speaking. Could be asking. One thing.

Step 2: Next week, do another. Small. Repeated small courage builds.

Step 3: You're not trying to be a hero. You're trying to be honest. One week at a time.

Step 1: The next time someone asks how you are, pause. Don't auto-respond with fine.

Step 2: Give a real answer. Even if it's just I'm struggling a bit.

Step 3: You're taking the opening instead of deflecting it. Real connection starts with real answers.
