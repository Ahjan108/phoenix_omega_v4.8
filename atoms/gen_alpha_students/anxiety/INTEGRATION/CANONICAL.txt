## INTEGRATION v01
---
mode: BODY_LANDED
reframe_type: BODY_FACT
weight: standard
carry_line: "Alarm firing is a signal, not a verdict."
---
Right now, your shoulders live somewhere up near your ears. Your chest feels smaller than it did this morning. The moment before you raise your hand in class—that's the alarm. It's not new. It's been running since you were small, since the first time you felt watched.

The alarm has a job. It's supposed to protect you. But it fires at the wrong things now—at speaking up, at being visible, at the moment before sending a text that matters. Your nervous system can't tell the difference between actual threat and social judgment. Both feel like they could end everything.

What I want you to know: that tightness in your chest. That's not weakness. That's your body doing exactly what it was designed to do. The problem isn't the alarm. It's that it's firing at things it shouldn't.

Alarm firing is a signal, not a verdict.

## INTEGRATION v02
---
mode: COST_VISIBLE

weight: standard

---
You've been keeping score. The group chat where you didn't reply. The moment in class when you had something to say and stayed silent. The version of yourself you show your friends versus the version that exists alone in your room at 2 AM.

Each time you shrink to avoid the alarm, you add something to an invisible ledger. It costs energy to stay small. It costs presence. It costs moments that could have been yours.

But here's what I've seen: the people who speak up—even while the alarm is firing—they're not less anxious. They're not braver in some inherent way. They've just decided that the cost of silence is higher than the cost of being heard, even imperfectly, even while shaking.

The cost of not speaking is its own kind of loud.

That being seen carries actual weight. That other people's judgment does matter, not because you're weak, but because you're human.

What if the real question isn't how to make the alarm stop. What if it's how to move forward while the alarm is firing. How to speak up while your hands shake. How to be visible while your nervous system is convinced that visibility is dangerous.

What if the alarm itself isn't the problem. What if the problem is believing that you can't do anything real until the alarm stops.

What if the alarm is not the problem.
