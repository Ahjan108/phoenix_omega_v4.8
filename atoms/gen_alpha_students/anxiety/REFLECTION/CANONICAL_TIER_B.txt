## REFLECTION v01
---
family: F1
voice_mode: teacher
mechanism_emphasis: automatic
tier_variant: B

Anxiety announces itself in the body before the mind understands why. The chest tightens, breath quickens, stomach turns. You are sitting in class, looking at an email from a teacher, thinking about a test three weeks away. Your nervous system has decided this is a threat. The alarm mechanism fires because it reads the stakes. It treats exposure the same way it treats actual danger. The mechanism cannot distinguish between a predator and a presentation. Both register as threats worthy of full activation. What you come to understand is that the alarm firing is not a sign something is actually dangerous. It is a sign your system has learned to perceive exposure as threat. The fire is real. The danger is not. You are carrying a sophisticated threat-detection system into an environment where the things it detects are not lethal.
---

## REFLECTION v02
---
family: F2
voice_mode: companion
mechanism_emphasis: invisible
tier_variant: B

Your nervous system is equipped with a threat-detection system built for a different world. Danger used to look like a predator or a fall. Now danger looks like a test, a presentation, the thought that you might get something wrong. The alarm fires with identical intensity whether the threat is physical or academic. You sit in class with a racing heart, convinced something is genuinely wrong. But the only wrong thing is that the mechanism is miscalibrated. The fire is real. The danger is not. The mechanism was designed to keep you alive. It is just designed for a world you do not live in. You carry sophistication into a place that needs responsiveness, not survival intensity. The alarm will keep firing because it is working exactly as designed.
