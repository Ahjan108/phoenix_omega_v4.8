I have noticed that anxiety often announces itself first in the body. The chest tightens. The breath becomes quick and shallow. The stomach turns. You are sitting in class, or looking at an email from a teacher, and your nervous system has decided that this is a threat. Not a real threat. Not something that will kill you. And the system keeps firing.

The feeling is so uncomfortable that the mind spins into solution-seeking mode. What can I do to make this stop. How can I fix this. What is the action that will make the discomfort go away. This solution-seeking mechanism is powerful and automatic.

I have noticed that anticipatory anxiety—the anxiety that lives in the future, not the present—has a particular grip on students. You are not anxious about what is happening right now. You are anxious about what might happen tomorrow, or next week, or in the test you have not taken yet. There is a part of your mind that is constantly running simulations. It is rehearsing scenarios. It is asking: what could go wrong.

I have noticed that the physical sensations of anxiety can become a source of anxiety themselves. Your heart races, and you notice it racing, and you become anxious about the racing heart, and then the racing intensifies. The mechanism catches itself in a feedback loop. What happens is this: the nervous system sends a signal. The body responds. The mind notices the response. The mind interprets the response as confirmation that something is wrong.

I have noticed that social anxiety in school operates through a very specific mechanism: the belief that people are paying much closer attention to you than they actually are. You make a mistake in class, say something awkward, miss a social cue, and you feel certain that everyone noticed. Everyone is remembering. Everyone is judging. But what I have come to observe is that everyone is also entirely focused on themselves. They are worried about their own mistakes.

mechanism_emphasis: automatic
tier_variant: D

I have noticed that comparison is one of the most powerful amplifiers of anxiety. You are anxious about your performance, and then you look at what someone else is doing, and you perceive that they are doing better, and the anxiety intensifies. They got an A, you got an A-minus. They answered the question confidently, you stumbled. They seem calm, you are not calm. This comparison mechanism is nearly involuntary. And the expansion of information is an expansion of anxiety.
