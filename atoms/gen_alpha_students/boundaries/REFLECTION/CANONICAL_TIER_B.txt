## REFLECTION v01
---
family: F1
voice_mode: teacher
mechanism_emphasis: protective
tier_variant: B

You cannot choose to have boundaries you have never experienced. The capacity develops through having it modeled, reinforced, and respected. If no one ever showed you what a boundary looks like, you learned a different architecture instead. Everything is available. Everything is negotiable. Everything can be demanded of you. The mechanism absorbed this lesson through watching the adults around you. When they said yes to everything, you learned yes was the only option. When they did not protect their own boundaries, they could not teach you to protect yours. Now when someone asks for something, your first response is yes. You do not even realize refusal was an option. The mechanism never developed the internal sense that says: this is mine, this is not mine. It only knows: everything is available.
---

## REFLECTION v02
---
family: F2
voice_mode: companion
mechanism_emphasis: inherited
tier_variant: B

Boundaries are not chosen. They are learned through experience. When someone violates your space and nothing happens to them, the mechanism learns: space is negotiable. When someone demands something of you and you are punished for refusing, the mechanism learns: refusal is not safe. When someone respects your no, the mechanism learns: no is possible. Many students did not grow up with modeling of healthy boundaries. There was no one who said no to the parents. There was no one who said no to the school. The adults in the environment were managing their own boundarylessness, not teaching boundaries. So the students learned boundarylessness instead. Now you do not know which requests are reasonable to deny. You do not know you are allowed to have preferences that differ from what others want. The mechanism never learned the distinction between what is yours and what is not.
