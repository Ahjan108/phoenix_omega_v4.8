## REFLECTION v01
---
family: F1
voice_mode: teacher
mechanism_emphasis: protective
tier_variant: C

I have noticed that boundaries are often presented as something you should have, as if they are a choice. In reality, the capacity to set a boundary is something that develops through having had boundaries modeled, reinforced, and respected. You cannot choose to have boundaries if you have never experienced them. There is a mechanism that learns what is negotiable and what is not negotiable through experience. When someone violates your space and nothing happens to them, the mechanism learns that space is negotiable. When someone demands something of you and you are punished for refusing, the mechanism learns that refusal is not safe. When someone respects your no, the mechanism learns that no is possible.

I have noticed that the inability to say no often coexists with people-pleasing. These are related but not identical. The inability to say no is about the absence of a boundary mechanism. The people-pleasing is about the mechanism that is trying to earn safety by being acceptable. What happens is this: you have learned that your safety, your belonging, your acceptability, depends on meeting the needs and wishes of other people. So the mechanism that evaluates situations includes a constant question: what does this person want from me. And the next question is: can I provide it. And if you can provide it, the mechanism says yes. The word no is not available.

mechanism_emphasis: protective
tier_variant: C

I have noticed that boundary-setting is often confused with selfishness. If you do not want to do something, if you want to protect your time or energy or space, the mechanism interprets that as selfishness. Not caring about others. Putting yourself first. Which is presented as the opposite of what a good person does. What I have come to understand is that the mechanism is operating under a false dichotomy. Either you are putting yourself first and are selfish, or you are putting others first and are good. But these are not the only options. You can care about other people and still have boundaries. You can be kind and generous and still refuse requests. These are not mutually exclusive.

I have noticed that the word boundary implies a line. But the reality of boundaries for people who have not had them modeled is more like a permeability. There is no clear line between self and other. Things flow in and out. The boundary is not firm. It is not even clear where the boundary is supposed to be. What I have come to understand is that the development of firm boundaries is usually a gradual process, and it is much harder when the permeability has been the norm. You have to simultaneously understand that a boundary is needed while you are still inside the system that created the permeability. The mechanism is learning through resistance rather than through support.
