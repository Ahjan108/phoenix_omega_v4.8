## REFLECTION v01
---
family: F1
voice_mode: teacher
mechanism_emphasis: protective
tier_variant: B

I have noticed that compassion fatigue often arrives quietly. You have been the person your friends text when things fall apart. You have been listening. You have been present. You have been the steady one. And then one day you realize that there is no one listening to you. That you are not present to yourself. That you are not steady anymore. You are running on empty. There is a mechanism that is called the caring collapse, and what it does is deplete the very capacity it is trying to sustain. You have spent your emotional energy on other people. You have given your attention to other people's problems. You have made room in your mind for other people's struggles. And the room you have made has taken up all the available space. What I have come to understand is that this mechanism is particularly active in environments where there is ambient suffering. Your friends are struggling. There is a pandemic. There is political instability. There is uncertainty about the future.

Your parent is unhappy, and understanding them will not make them happy. Your classmate is lonely, and your friendship will not solve the loneliness. That if you just hold it all with enough attention, something will shift. That your presence will heal. But the presence does not heal. The people still hurt. The problems still exist. The suffering continues. What I have come to understand is that the mechanism that believes this is often responding to a very deep human desire: the desire to help. The desire to alleviate suffering. The desire to make a difference. But the mechanism has attached that desire to an impossible outcome. You cannot heal other people through listening. You can only listen.

Unsustainable caring burns you out and leaves you unable to care for anyone, including yourself.
