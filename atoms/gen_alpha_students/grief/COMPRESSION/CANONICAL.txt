## COMPRESSION v01
---
compression_family: C1
---
Someone died and the world is supposed to go on. It does. School continues. People talk. Time moves. But inside you are completely still. The still doesn't make sense because the world is moving. That collision—between the still inside and the moving outside—is grief. And you don't have to pretend the world hasn't moved. You can be still and let the world move. Both things are true.
---

## COMPRESSION v02
---
compression_family: C2
---
Grief shows up as waves. One moment you're fine. The next moment you're drowning. A song comes on, a smell hits you, someone mentions their name, and suddenly it's back. You're not broken for having the waves. The waves are how grief works. They get smaller over time. But they might always come back sometimes. That's okay.
---

## COMPRESSION v03
---
compression_family: C3
---
You're supposed to be "over it" by now. People expect you to be fine. But grief doesn't have a schedule. It doesn't care what people expect. Your grief is the right length. Your grief is the right intensity. Everyone else's timeline is irrelevant. You grieve how you grieve.
---

## COMPRESSION v04
---
compression_family: C4
---
You feel guilty for laughing. For having fun. For moving forward. Like if you're happy, you're betraying the person who died. But that's not true. The person who died would want you to laugh. Would want you to live. Moving forward is not betrayal. It's honoring the life they lived by actually living yours.
---

## COMPRESSION v05
---
compression_family: C5
---
You're angry. You're angry at the person for dying. You're angry at God or the universe or whoever is responsible. You're angry at people who are still alive. That anger is part of grief. It's not bad. It's not wrong. It's energy looking for a place to go. Let it be there. It will settle.
---

## COMPRESSION v06
---
compression_family: C1
---
Nobody really gets it. They say "I'm sorry for your loss" like it's a phrase instead of a reality. They try to make you feel better. They offer platitudes. But there's nothing to fix. There's just this: someone important is gone. The relief comes when you find people who can sit with the gone instead of trying to fix it.
---

## COMPRESSION v07
---
compression_family: C2
---
You keep forgetting they're dead. You think of something you want to tell them and then you remember. That forgetting and remembering happens over and over. It's not disrespect. It's how your brain processes. Eventually the forgetting gets longer. But it might never completely stop. And that's okay.

## COMPRESSION v08
---
compression_family: C3
---
You're having trouble sleeping or concentrating or being present. Your system is in shock. Your nervous system is trying to process something your mind can't quite grasp. Be gentle with yourself. You're not failing. You're grieving. Let yourself be incompetent for a while. You'll come back.

## COMPRESSION v09
---
compression_family: C4
---
Grief is making you question everything. What's the point of anything if people we love just die? Why does anything matter? These questions are real and they matter. But they don't have easy answers. The point is both everything and nothing. You have to learn to live in that contradiction.

---
compression_family: C1
---
You're scared you're going to forget what they looked like. What their voice sounded like. What their laugh sounded like. That fear is part of grief. But forgetting the details doesn't mean forgetting the person. The essence stays. You'll carry them differently over time. That's not loss. That's integration.

compression_family: C2
---
You want things to go back to normal but you also know normal is gone. The before was better. The after is different. There's no going back. The adjustment to that loss—the loss of the "before" on top of the loss of the person—is real. You're grieving the past and the future at the same time.

Grief is making you aware of how fragile everything is. How quickly people disappear. How precarious life is. That awareness is real and it's scary. But it's also clarifying. Things matter more. People matter more. Time matters more. That's grief's gift: perspective on what actually matters.
