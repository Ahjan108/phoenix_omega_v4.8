Maya's dog dies on a Tuesday. She goes to school on Wednesday. The school doesn't know. The hallway is the same hallway. The lockers are the same lockers. The world didn't rearrange itself around the fact that Max is gone. Maya expected—without knowing she expected it—that something would be different. Nothing is different. Max died yesterday and fractions continue. The continuation is the grief's landscape—everything continues except the thing that ended.

Maya discovers the grief comes in waves. Not constant—intermittent. She's fine in English class. Then she opens her backpack and finds one of Max's dog hairs on her notebook. The hair triggers a wave. The wave peaks: tears, tight throat, the image of Max on his bed. The wave recedes: she's back in English class, the teacher is talking, the notebook is open. The waves are the grief's rhythm. The rhythm doesn't follow a schedule. The rhythm follows triggers—sensory details that connect to the lost thing. The hair on the notebook is a sensory bridge between current reality and the reality that ended. The bridge crosses without warning.

Tyler discovers the divorce grief has layers. Layer one: the family structure changing. Layer two: the loss of certainty—if the family can change, what else can change. Layer three: the guilt—wondering if he caused it, knowing intellectually he didn't, feeling it emotionally anyway. Layer four: the ambiguity—his parents are both alive and present but the thing that held them together is gone. Ambiguous loss is harder to process than clear loss because clear loss has a defined object. Tyler's loss has no clear object. He didn't lose his parents. He lost their togetherness. The togetherness was invisible and essential and its absence is everywhere.
