I'm calling it the squeeze mechanism because that's what happens in your body when the threat is coming from both directions at once. Your mother's doctor calls about her latest scan. Your daughter's college emails say the financial aid package isn't enough. Your body doesn't distinguish between them. There's no single source to face, no one problem to solve. The squeeze is the simultaneity itself—the refusal of the system to let you focus on just one crisis before the other one demands attention. You know this already if you're sitting in your late forties or early fifties. You've felt it in your chest at 3 a.m., the way your nervous system stays activated because it can't pick a threat to worry about first. The amygdala doesn't care about your organizational skills. It just knows that the pressure is coming from both above and below, and there's no exit route. I learned that the squeeze isn't actually about the problems themselves—plenty of previous generations have faced elder care or child expenses. It's about the simultaneity with no narrative of temporary.
