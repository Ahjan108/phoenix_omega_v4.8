I'm watching you hold the line between yourself and the people you care for, and the line is barely there. What you call boundaries is actually more like a membrane that lets everything through except your own needs. You know this mechanism—the way your sense of worth is constructed entirely from what you provide, the way stopping feels like disappearing. The provider identity doesn't arrive as a conscious choice. It emerges automatically from years of being needed, from early learning that your value is in your usefulness, from the particular reward structure of being the capable one. By the time you hit your fifties, the mechanism is so integrated that you don't even think of it as a mechanism. You think of it as character. As who you are. You know what happens when you try to set boundaries. You feel selfish. Not in a theoretical way, but in your body—a guilt that pulls at your chest, a sense that you're failing at the one job that actually matters.
