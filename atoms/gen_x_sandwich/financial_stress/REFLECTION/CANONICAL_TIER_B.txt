I'm calling it the retirement math that doesn't close because that's what it is. A number that moves every time you recalculate it. An equation that has too many variables and not enough resources to solve for them. The mechanism operates automatically because the math doesn't reward figuring it out. The math only gets worse when you look at it. The retirement math that doesn't close is not a failure of planning. You've probably been saving since your twenties. You've probably made decent money over your career. You've probably been doing everything right from a financial perspective. And yet the number that shows up at the end—the amount you'll need to live on for thirty years after you stop working—that number is still bigger than what you'll have. You know this. You've probably felt it when you looked at the numbers. That moment of recognition that the equation doesn't work. That moment of awareness that no amount of your remaining working years will actually get you to the number that the calculations say you need.
