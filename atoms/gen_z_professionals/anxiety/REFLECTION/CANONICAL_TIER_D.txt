What I did not understand for a long time is that burnout does not announce itself. It does not arrive the way I expected — with a clear breaking point, a day when I finally could not continue. It arrived as a gradual narrowing. The things that used to feel interesting started feeling neutral. The things that felt neutral started feeling heavy. I kept performing the same outputs. From the outside, nothing had changed.

Something I have come to think is true: the body keeps a more honest ledger than the calendar does. The calendar shows productivity. It shows utilization. It shows that the time was accounted for. The body is tracking something else — a running total of what was spent and what came back. When those two numbers diverge far enough, for long enough, the body starts reporting the discrepancy in the only language it has. Tension. Flatness.

I want to talk about the specific exhaustion of performing fine. Not performing poorly — that would be easier to locate, easier to name. I mean the exhaustion of continuing to produce the expected outputs, hit the expected marks, show up in the expected ways, while the interior experience of doing so has quietly become something else. This is one of the more disorienting features of burnout for people who are good at their work. The competence does not leave.

## REFLECTION v09

Something that took me time to understand is the difference between being busy and being depleted. They can look identical from the outside — full calendar, constant output, no apparent space. But they are not the same state, and they do not require the same response. Busy is a condition of volume. There is a lot to do and you are doing it. Depleted is a condition of resource. The supply that the doing draws from has run low.

I have noticed that burnout often arrives with a companion feeling that is easy to mistake for its cause: the sense that you should be able to handle this. That other people handle more. That what you are feeling is a weakness or a limitation rather than a signal. This comparison is almost never accurate, because it compares your interior experience to other people's visible outputs. Their interior experience is not visible. You do not know what it costs them.

There is something specific that happens when ambition and exhaustion occupy the same body. The ambition does not disappear. This is one of the things that makes the state hard to name. If the ambition had left, the picture would be clearer. But it stays. It continues to generate plans, goals, ideas about what should come next. Meanwhile the exhaustion is also there, making each of those plans feel heavier than it used to. The maintenance is what got missed.

One of the harder things to hold is that you can be grateful and depleted at the same time. That the job can be a good job — better than alternatives, better than what you expected — and also be taking more than it is returning. Gratitude and depletion are not opposites. They can coexist. But our culture tends to treat them as mutually exclusive: if you are grateful, the depletion is illegitimate.

One of the things I have learned is that the question "are you okay." is almost never answered honestly by someone who is burning out. Not because they are lying, exactly. But because by the time burnout is well established, the answer "I'm fine" has become automatic — faster than any actual assessment of whether fine is accurate. This automaticity is not laziness or evasiveness. It is a survival behavior. In most environments, not being fine has costs.

Something I return to often is the question of what we mean when we say someone is strong. In most workplace contexts, strength means continuing. Showing up regardless of what is happening internally. Not letting the internal state interfere with the external performance. This is one definition. It is not the only one. One of the less-discussed features of burnout is what it does to the relationship with other people. Not just the withdrawal — the way social energy decreases and interactions start to cost more than they return. But something more specific: the sense that what you are experiencing is not legible to the people around you, and that making it legible would require more than you currently have. So you maintain the surface in social situations too.
