Jordan rehearses saying no. In the shower, in the car, on the walk to the office. The words are simple: "I can't take that on right now." Six words. His body responds to the rehearsal as though the refusal is already happening. Heart rate climbs. Palms dampen. Jaw sets. By the time the actual request comes, his body has run the alarm so many times that saying yes feels like relief. The alarm fires on the anticipation of the boundary, not the boundary itself. He says yes before the alarm can peak again. The alarm trained him to avoid its own activation.

COST_TYPE: internal
COST_INTENSITY: 3
IDENTITY_STAGE: destabilization
---
Jordan maps the alarm's math. Setting a boundary: anticipated cost (catastrophe, rejection, isolation) versus actual cost (brief discomfort, usually no consequence). The alarm always calculates the anticipated cost. The actual cost only becomes visible after the boundary is set. The alarm prevents the boundary, which prevents the data, which prevents the correction. It's a closed loop: the alarm stays calibrated to catastrophe because the catastrophe is never tested. Every time he says yes to avoid the alarm, the alarm's miscalibration is preserved. His compliance is the alarm's maintenance system.

## STORY v21
---
path: reading threat everywhere
BAND: 1
SEMANTIC_FAMILY: SF1
---
I'm starting to notice something strange about how I respond to things. A message from my boss arrives at 3pm and my nervous system spikes. Is this about me? Is this a problem? Did I do something wrong? I read the message three times. It's just a question about a project timeline. My brain has already created a whole story where I failed. The false alarm is quiet at first. It's just a little tightening. Just a small surge of "what if." But it's constantly happening. Someone sends a message. My nervous system reads it as a threat. I have to manually check the actual content. Is it actually a problem or did my body create the problem? Most of the time it's my body creating it. But my body doesn't care. The false alarm says: threat detected. Act now. Figure it out later. By the time I realize there's no threat, my heart is already racing. And I'm exhausted from the constant false mobilization. The system is supposed to protect me. Instead it's consuming my energy with phantom dangers. I'm learning to distrust my own body because my body is lying.
