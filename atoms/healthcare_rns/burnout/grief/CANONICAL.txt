## STORY v01
---
path: the patient you couldn't save
BAND: 1
SEMANTIC_FAMILY: SF1
---
I remember her name. Room 412, day shift, and she was talking about her grandchildren when I checked the monitors at 0700. By 1530 her oxygen was tanking and the team was calling the code before I could even explain what was happening to her daughter. I knew the anatomy of her decline. I could trace it on the waveforms. But I didn't see her gradient shifting. I talked about her labs to the resident like she wasn't there. Two weeks later I'm in the break room and I can't remember if I actually held her hand or if I only meant to. The idealism you have when you start nursing—that you'll be present for people at their worst—gets replaced by the efficiency of moving through the shift. You learn to see the pathology instead of the person. And then sometimes the pathology wins anyway, and you realize you spent the last hours of someone's life being clinical instead of human. That's the grief that doesn't have a name in the job. It's not dramatic. It's just a slow recognition that you're not the nurse you thought you'd be.

## STORY v02
---
path: the colleague who left
BAND: 2
SEMANTIC_FAMILY: SF2
---
Marcus was on the unit for six years. One of the ones who actually seemed okay with it. He'd laugh in the break room, not the dark kind of laugh but something that felt genuine. He talked about his kids. He had the schedule dialed in. And then one day his name was off the roster. Not transferred to another unit. Just gone. I heard through someone who heard through someone that he'd taken a job in pharmaceutical sales. Started making actual money. I see him sometimes at the grocery store and he looks lighter somehow. Younger. Like he'd been carrying something on his shoulders that I couldn't see until he wasn't wearing it anymore. And now when I'm charting past midnight or scrubbing a code out of my head before I can sleep, I think about what it would feel like to walk away from this. To just stop showing up to witness other people's suffering. The unit lost three nurses last month. The ratios got worse. They hired new grads who have the old idealism still intact. And I know that in a few years they'll either leave like Marcus or they'll stay and something in them will go quiet. I'm still here, which means I chose this. That's the grief I can't articulate to anyone.

## STORY v03
---
path: the nurse you used to be
BAND: 3
SEMANTIC_FAMILY: SF3
---
I have video from my first shift as a grad. My preceptor taped it as a joke. I'm watching every single patient interaction like it's sacred. I'm explaining things to people. I'm looking them in the eye. My hands are shaking but my intention is completely clear. I wanted to matter. I wanted to show people that someone cared about their experience in this terrible moment. Fifteen years later I'm efficient. I'm fast. I know which patients will sue, which families will be reasonable, which MDs don't want to be bothered with clinical assessments from nurses. I moved three admission intakes and a discharge paperwork through my section in forty-five minutes. Did them all well. Didn't miss anything clinically. But I didn't know any of their names by the end of the shift. The nurse in that video would be horrified by me. And I can't get her back because she was inefficient. She couldn't have survived the caseloads we have now. So she had to die so that I could function. That's the grief nobody talks about. It's not about losing a patient. It's about losing yourself in small increments and calling it professionalism.

## STORY v04
---
path: the system that broke the idealism
BAND: 4
SEMANTIC_FAMILY: SF4
---
You're taught in school that nursing is a calling. That you enter the field because you want to care for people. Because you have a gift for compassion. And for a while you believe it. You believe that the long hours and the physical strain and the emotional weight are worth it because you're making a difference. But at some point you realize the system isn't designed for you to make that difference. It's designed to move bodies through the hospital as efficiently as possible. You are a task completion engine. You are a liability mitigation system. You are the thing between the hospital and a lawsuit. The patients are secondary. The actual wellbeing of your patients is secondary to the documentation and the metrics and the pressures from the C-suite. And once you see that, you can't unsee it. You can't go back to believing that you're doing this for noble reasons. You're doing it because you need insurance and because you've invested eight years in this credential and because leaving would mean admitting that you were wrong about what nursing is. So you grieve the version of yourself that still believed. You grieve the profession that you thought you were entering. And you show up the next shift because that's what broken idealism looks like. It looks like consistency. It looks like professionalism. It looks like a nurse who doesn't expect anymore.

## STORY v05
---
path: the codes you carry
BAND: 5
SEMANTIC_FAMILY: SF5
---
I can list them. The thirty-four-year-old who coded in the morning and didn't make it. The eighty-two-year-old who we brought back but she never really came back. The teenager who shouldn't have been there in the first place. And for each one there's a specific thing I can't unhear or unsee. The sound of ribs cracking during compressions. The silence after someone's already gone but you keep pushing chest. The family member's voice changing between hope and disbelief. I used to process these. I'd go to the break room and someone would make a dark joke and I'd laugh because it's the only way to stay sane. But somewhere along the line the jokes stopped working. Now I just carry them. I carry the feeling that I should have done something different even though I know I did everything right. I carry the certainty that the next code will go the same way and the one after that. The grief isn't pure anymore. It's mixed with exhaustion and futility and resentment that I'm spending my life seeing people die in ways that feel increasingly pointless. I used to think grief meant you cared. Now I know grief is what happens when you've cared so much and so often that caring itself becomes a pathology. It becomes the thing that will eventually kill your license or your mind or both.
