My hands are doing something. I watch them move through a dressing change. They know how to do this. Unwrap the gauze. Assess the wound. Clean it. Document the appearance. Redress it. I can't pinpoint exactly when it happened. I just know at some point the separation occurred. My body continues to perform. My clinical judgment is still there. But me. The person. That left a while ago. And I keep showing up anyway because the work needs to be done and my hands know how to do it even if my consciousness is already clocked out.

I'm typing a narrative note about Mr. Hendricks. Describing his mental status. Documenting his responsiveness. His family is sitting by his bedside looking hopeful and I can see the clinical reality. His trajectory isn't good. The labs suggest deterioration. The exam findings are consistent with decline. I'm charting the objective facts while the part of me that might feel compassion for his family is just shut off. It's like I've dissociated from the emotional content. I'm transcribing what I observe into the system without actually processing what it means. I know clinically that this is sad. I can name the pathophysiology. I can anticipate the course. But I'm typing about it with the emotional engagement of someone ordering supplies. The family asks me how he's doing and I give them the professional response. I explain the plan. I use appropriate tone. I'm doing everything right on the outside. Inside I'm empty. I'm just moving information from one place to another. I'm not connecting with the experience anymore. I did that once. I remember crying in the break room about a patient. Now I'm charting the decline of someone and feeling nothing. Is this detachment? Is this self-protection? Is this what happens when you've held too many hands that didn't make it? I don't know anymore. I just know I'm doing excellent clinical work while fundamentally not present for any of it.

## STORY v03

path: the dissociation that looks like professionalism
BAND: 3
SEMANTIC_FAMILY: SF3
---
I can perform. That's what I've learned. I can show up and be competent even when I'm not actually in my body. My preceptor from orientation used to say that experience means you don't have to think about the task anymore. You can respond automatically. That's true. But somewhere along the way the automaticity expanded to include my entire emotional response. I'm watching myself work. It's like I'm behind glass. I can see the nurse doing the right things. Making good decisions. Catching errors before they become problems. But she's separate from me. She's the version that got forged in twelve-hour shifts and code blues and family meetings at the end of life. She's competent. She's unshakeable. She's also not actually there. I have conversations and don't remember them. I document interactions that feel like they happened to someone else. I drive home after a shift and can't recall the route. This is what dissociation looks like in professional form. I'm functional. I'm excellent at what I do. I'm just not present while doing it. The strange part is that everyone sees exactly what they want to see. The hospital sees a reliable nurse. The patients see someone who cares. My family sees someone who's preoccupied. Nobody sees the fact that the person is gone. I'm just executing protocols. And the tragedy is that this is sustainable. I could do this until retirement. I could be a ghost for thirty years.

## STORY v04
---
path: the night you realize you can't feel anything
BAND: 4
SEMANTIC_FAMILY: SF4
---
I was charting around 1730. The shift was almost over. I got a text that my brother had had an accident. Bad one. Potential spinal injury. I called my mom and she was crying. And I was still charting. Still documenting the afternoon assessment. I told her I'd meet them at the hospital after my shift. I finished all my notes. I did the handoff to night shift. I clocked out. Drove to the other hospital. And I was numb the entire time. I know what I felt should be panic. My brother is in an imaging center with a potential permanent injury. The clinical outcomes for this type of trauma are not good. But I was stuck in observation mode. I was watching myself be concerned instead of being concerned. I was monitoring my vital signs like he was a patient. Assessing my own response for accuracy instead of actually experiencing it. I was thinking about the mechanisms of spinal trauma instead of what it meant for my family. And the worst part is that I could see what was happening. I could understand that my brain had learned to shut off emotional processing as a survival mechanism. And I couldn't turn it back on. I was completely dissociated from the worst moment of my family's year and I couldn't access the part of me that should have been devastated. That's when I understood that the watcher thing isn't just about work anymore. I've split myself so completely that I'm not sure I can function any other way now.

I don't know when I stopped actually living and started just moving through the days. The watcher was fine at work. Essential, actually. But then it followed me home. I'm sitting with my kids and I'm present but not there. I'm making dinner while thinking about the shift. I'm in a conversation with my partner and I'm responding appropriately but I'm not actually engaging. I've become a shell that performs life. I show up. I do the right things. I say the right things. But the person doing them isn't actually inside the body anymore. I tried therapy. The therapist kept asking me what I was feeling and I genuinely couldn't access it. I could describe the emotions that should be there. I could explain the psychology. But I couldn't feel them. I'm dissociated from my own existence. And the clock-watching thing that kept me functional at work has now consumed everything. I watch myself parent. I watch myself love my family. I watch myself try and fail to sleep because the watch never turns off. I'm exhausted from performing humanity. I'm exhausted from not being able to actually live it. I tried calling in sick. I lasted two days before the emptiness got so complete that I had to go back to work. At least there I'm supposed to be a watcher. At least there the dissociation has a function. But at home it's just a woman living in her own life like she's viewing it through a camera. And I don't know how to fix this. I don't know if I want to fix it. Maybe this is just who I am now.
