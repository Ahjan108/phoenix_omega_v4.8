Nora's patient is crying. The crying is real—pain, fear, isolation. Nora stands at the bedside and can identify each component of the distress. She can name it. She can chart it. She cannot feel it. The inability to feel the patient's distress arrives without announcement. She remembers feeling it—the first year, the second year. The feeling was automatic, like breathing. Now the feeling requires effort she doesn't have at hour ten of a twelve-hour shift with five other patients waiting. The effort shortage isn't callousness. The effort shortage is a tank running empty. The tank was full once. The tank has been draining for seven years with no refill protocol.

Nora maps the depletion pattern. Each shift requires emotional output—empathy, patience, presence—for every patient interaction. The output is not discretionary. It's the job. The problem is the input. There is no scheduled emotional recovery. There's no refill between patients, between shifts, between crises. Physical recovery has protocols: breaks, days off, vacation. Emotional recovery has nothing. The system treats emotional capacity as infinite. The system designs staffing ratios for clinical tasks and assumes the emotional labor is free. The labor is not free. The labor draws from a finite reservoir. Seven years of drawing without replenishment produced the emptiness at the bedside.
