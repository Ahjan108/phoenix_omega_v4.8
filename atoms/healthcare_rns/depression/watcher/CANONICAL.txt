---

COST_TYPE: social
COST_INTENSITY: 2
IDENTITY_STAGE: pre_awareness
---
Nora is charting. The shift is five hours in. She has completed four medication administrations, responded to two call lights, communicated with two physicians, and updated the family of one patient on the clinical trajectory. The work is technically correct. Her fingers move through the charting with the muscle memory of five years. Her voice in the phone call with the resident was clear and organized. Her assessment of the patient trending toward respiratory decline was accurate. But something inside the execution is absent. She is not experiencing the execution. She is watching the execution. She notices her own hand moving through the mouse clicks. She hears her own voice explaining the vital signs. The video of Nora as a nurse is playing correctly. Nora as a viewer of the video is somewhere behind the glass. The separation is so clean that she sits for a moment wondering if the separation is normal. It feels new. It also feels like it might be fine. The patients are safe. The work is being done. Maybe this is what professional distance is supposed to feel like.

The patient says the pain is better. Aisha feels the relief that should accompany this. The relief does not arrive. Instead, there is a kind of neutrality. She performed the task. She stands at the bedside and realizes that the patient is feeling better because of actions that moved through her hands and brain. But those actions feel like they belong to someone else. She is observing the nursing that is happening. She is not the nurse that the nursing is happening through. This realization is so strange that she sits with it. The patient is fine. The work is correct. Maybe the distance is a feature of being experienced. Maybe five years in, this is what the work is supposed to feel like.

## STORY v21
---
path: observing your own flatness
BAND: 1
SEMANTIC_FAMILY: SF1
---
Nora is watching herself not feel. She's become an observer of her own numbness. A patient thanked her today and she felt nothing. She noted the nothing-feeling. She watched herself register the nothing. She has become bifurcated—one part doing the nursing, one part observing that the nursing part is empty. The observer is noticing: you are numb. The doing part continues. The observing part catalogs the numbness. This split between doing and observing is the beginning of depression-dissociation. She's not integrated. She's split into performer and audience. The audience watches the performer go through empty motions. The split is efficient in the short term. In the long term, the split prevents healing. You can't reconnect to what you're observing from a distance.
