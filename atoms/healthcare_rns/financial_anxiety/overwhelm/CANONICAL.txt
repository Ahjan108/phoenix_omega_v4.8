## STORY v01
---
path: the debt that doesn't get smaller
BAND: 1
SEMANTIC_FAMILY: SF1
---
I owe 147 thousand dollars. That number is approximate. It used to be higher but I've been paying for seven years and it barely moved for the first five. The interest alone costs me more per month than some people's rent. I have a master's degree. I make seventy-two thousand a year. By the time I pay taxes and student loans and the cost of living in a city with a hospital that pays that salary, I have nothing left. My nursing school was fourteen thousand a semester. That seemed reasonable at the time. My mom co-signed. I was going to make good money. Nursing was supposed to be stable. Supposed to be respectable. Supposed to be an investment that paid off. And it did pay off compared to people without degrees. But the debt is crushing. I can't buy a house. I can't save. I can't plan beyond the next paycheck. I turned down a promotion because it would have changed my student loan repayment plan and I'd actually pay more. So I'm stuck in a job that doesn't compensate me adequately because the system is designed to keep people with my debt in permanent financial instability. The math of this is that I'll be paying this until I'm fifty-eight years old. That assumes I never miss a payment. That assumes no emergency depletes my savings. That assumes I keep this job. The weight of that number is constant.

## STORY v02
---
path: the choice between work and health
BAND: 2
SEMANTIC_FAMILY: SF2
---
My knee is bad. The MRI shows cartilage damage. The orthopedic surgeon says I need surgery. But surgery means time off work. Nursing doesn't have paid disability. I have ten days of PTO per year. After that I'm on unpaid leave or I have to come back injured and try to move through twelve-hour shifts on medications that shouldn't be operating heavy machinery. The surgery itself is covered by insurance. The six weeks of recovery isn't. I did the math. I can't afford to have my knee fixed. So I'm limping through shifts. I'm taking ibuprofen that's destroying my stomach lining. My doctor asked if I was managing the pain and I told her the truth. I can't afford to manage it. I can't afford to fix it. I can afford to destroy my body more slowly. That's the choice. I've already had two surgeries for work injuries. Compressed disc from patient transfers. Repetitive strain in my wrist. Both I tried to manage without surgery. Both eventually required intervention anyway. But the cost of that recovery took months to absorb financially. I'm thirty-seven. I can feel the physical toll of this job accumulating. And I can't afford to stop. I can't afford to fix anything. So I'm just managing decline. I'm choosing which parts of my body I can afford to let deteriorate. That's not sustainable. But sustainability isn't the point. Survival is. I need this job. I need the health insurance it provides. Even though the insurance doesn't actually cover the things I need to stay healthy enough to keep working.

## STORY v03

path: the math that doesn't add up
BAND: 3
SEMANTIC_FAMILY: SF3
---
I live in a city where studios go for eighteen hundred a month. I make six thousand a month before taxes. After taxes I'm at approximately four thousand two hundred. My student loans take five hundred and twenty. My car payment is three hundred and fifty. Insurance is two hundred. Gas is one hundred fifty. That's eleven hundred and twenty. I have three thousand and eighty left. Rent takes eighteen hundred. I have one thousand two hundred eighty for everything else. Food. Utilities. Phone. Internet. Medical costs. Medication. Dentist. Optometrist. Emergencies. Clothing. That's three hundred and eighty per month for everything that isn't housing or debt. I haven't bought new scrubs in two years. My shoes are worn through. I cut my own hair now. I don't eat out. I don't see movies. I don't have hobbies. I'm thirty-seven years old and I'm budgeting like I'm in debt crisis. Actually I am in debt crisis. I'm just calling it life. The crazy part is that I'm doing better than most people. This is upper-middle-class debt. This is a professional salary. And I still can't figure out how to exist financially. I work in healthcare. I took out loans to get the credentials to do it. The system is supposed to value that. But the salary hasn't kept up with inflation or with the cost of education. So I'm trapped in a job I'm increasingly burning out on because I can't afford to leave. I can't afford to go back to school for something else. I can't afford to take time off. I just have to absorb the overwhelm and keep working until something breaks. Either the job or me.

## STORY v04
---
path: the shift that cost more than it paid
BAND: 4
SEMANTIC_FAMILY: SF4
---
I picked up an extra shift last month. Twelve hours of overtime. That's supposed to help financially. The math looked good. I made about two hundred and twenty dollars gross. After taxes it was approximately one hundred sixty. But I had to pay the babysitter to cover the extra hours. That's one hundred and forty dollars. My car broke down on the way to that shift. The repair was eight hundred dollars. So I picked up one extra shift and it ended up costing me six hundred and forty dollars. That's the overwhelming part. I'm working more and getting further behind. The only way to survive financially is to maximize hours. But the cost of maximizing hours includes babysitting, transportation, food, stress management, and the accelerated physical breakdown of working seventy hours a week instead of forty. I calculated once what my actual hourly wage is if you factor in the unpaid lunch breaks, the unpaid charting time, the commute, the childcare, the healthcare costs from the physical strain, the mental health support I need because of the job stress. My real hourly wage is probably around twenty-two dollars. I could make more working at a tech company entry level without the physical and emotional devastation. But I've already invested eight years in this career. The sunken cost fallacy is keeping me locked in. I'm working more hours to pay debt for the education that should have been the investment that freed me. Instead it's the thing that ensures I stay trapped.

## STORY v05
---
path: the financial overwhelm that becomes life
BAND: 5
SEMANTIC_FAMILY: SF5
---
I don't even think about money anymore. That's how overwhelming it's become. I've crossed from awareness into numbness. I don't check my balance. I don't open the bills from the credit card I had to use for emergency repairs. I know I'm falling further behind and I'm just accepting it. My therapist says I'm in financial avoidance and that's a sign of depression. But avoidance is rational. What is checking the numbers going to do? I'm going to see that I'm insolvent. That my debt is growing even though I'm paying on it. That I'll never actually be ahead. That my kids will graduate into a world where they'll either go to expensive schools and get saddled with debt like me or they'll go into lower-wage work without credentials and live the same struggle I did. I'm trapped in a cycle that has no exit. So I work. I work double shifts sometimes. I skip meals because I can't afford to eat enough. I'm losing weight from the stress and malnutrition. My doctor is concerned. But I can't afford to not work. I can't afford to get sick. I can't afford to slow down. The financial overwhelm has become so complete that it's now just the baseline of my existence. I'm anxious about money constantly. I'm thinking about it even when I'm asleep. It's invaded everything. And there's no solution. Just more work. Just more exhaustion. Just more years of this until maybe I die with the debt paid off or maybe I die with it still hanging over everything I was supposed to accomplish.
