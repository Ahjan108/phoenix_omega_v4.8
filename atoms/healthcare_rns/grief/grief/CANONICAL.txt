Derek counts his patient deaths. Seventeen in three years. Not all unexpected—some were comfort care, some were long declines. But seventeen people whose last professional caregiver was Derek. Seventeen people whose final vital signs he documented, whose families he stood with, whose bodies he prepared for transport. The count produces a weight that surprises him. He hasn't counted before. The deaths happened individually, spaced across months. Individually, each was processed and filed. Collectively, seventeen is a number that sits differently in the body than any single death. The accumulation was invisible until the counting made it visible. The visibility produces a grief he didn't know was waiting.

Nora identifies the grief's location: it lives in the personal details she absorbs about patients. The clinical facts—diagnoses, labs, medications—process through professional channels and are metabolized by training. The personal details—the blanket triangle, the daughter's name, the coffee preference—process through human channels that nursing doesn't equip or support. Aisha identifies the unnamed grief as a category error. The grief categories available to her are: personal grief (family, friends) and clinical grief (expected patient outcomes, professional processing). Derek examines what happens to unprocessed accumulated grief. Seventeen deaths, individually processed as clinical events, collectively unprocessed as a body of loss. The unprocessed collective manifests as: fatigue that sleep doesn't fix, a growing emotional distance from new patients, a reluctance to learn personal details about the people in his care.
