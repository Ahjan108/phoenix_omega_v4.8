## STORY v01
---
path: the nurse who always seems certain
BAND: 1
SEMANTIC_FAMILY: SF1
---
There's a nurse on my unit named Sarah. She's been here for eight years. She moves through the shift like she's not thinking about it. She makes clinical decisions in seconds. She handles family drama without losing her composure. She's calm when things go wrong. And everyone gravitates toward her. When patients ask for someone they ask for Sarah. When new staff needs guidance they go to Sarah. She seems to know everything. She seems to never second-guess herself. And I'm still here thinking through every decision. I'm double-checking my work. I'm asking questions even though I know I should probably know the answers by now. I'm walking out of patient rooms wondering if I explained things clearly enough. And Sarah just moves forward like it's all obvious. I wonder if she ever doubted herself. I wonder if she ever went home and replayed a conversation trying to figure out if she'd given the right advice. Probably not. She seems too confident for that. So I'm convinced there's something about her that I don't have. Some inherent quality that makes nursing natural for her and not for me. I'm comparing myself to her and I'm losing. And that makes me question whether I actually belong in this profession at all.

## STORY v02
---
path: the colleague who's never rattled
BAND: 2
SEMANTIC_FAMILY: SF2
---
A family member was aggressive toward me the other day. They questioned every decision the team was making. They accused me of not knowing what I was doing. And they were right in a way. The plan was complicated and I wasn't completely certain of the rationale. So their challenge landed. I left the room feeling small and incompetent. I told myself that I'd study the case more. But the real response was shame. Meanwhile a colleague had a similar situation. The family pushed back on her assessment and she pushed back on the family. She explained her thinking with such confidence that she essentially ended the argument by force of certainty. She wasn't more educated than me. She wasn't right and I wasn't. But she had the confidence to hold her position and I didn't. She just knew. Or at least she acted like she knew. And that's what seems to matter. The ability to perform certainty. The ability to not show doubt. And I don't have that. I show uncertainty. I express doubt. I ask clarifying questions. All of which are good things clinically. But they make me seem less competent than a colleague who's doing the same work but doing it with more confidence. I'm being compared against her confidence, not against her actual clinical judgment. And I'm failing the comparison.

## STORY v03
---
path: the attending who makes you smaller
BAND: 3
SEMANTIC_FAMILY: SF3
---
Dr. Patel asked me about a patient's labs this morning. Simple question. Just wanted to know if the albumin was improving. I should know this. I spent an hour on this patient this morning. I checked the labs. I noted the trends. But when he asked me I blanked. The words didn't come. I stammered through an answer that was technically correct but delivered with no confidence. And he looked at me like I'd failed him. Like the fact that I could recall the information but couldn't deliver it with certainty meant I hadn't actually learned it. Afterward I kept thinking about how he looked at me. How he seemed disappointed. How he seemed to think I was less competent than the other nurses on the unit. And I started comparing myself to how I seem when I'm around him versus how I seem when I'm with other staff. I'm smaller with him. I'm less sure. I'm more likely to defer. And I think it's because somewhere along the line I got the message that he thinks I'm not at his level. That I'm not actually competent enough to practice nursing at the standard he expects. So when I'm around him I perform that incompetence. I become what I think he sees when he looks at me. And I don't know if that's real or if I've just internalized his judgment so completely that I've become it.

## STORY v04
---
path: the shift where you realize you're the worst one here
BAND: 4
SEMANTIC_FAMILY: SF4
---
I was assigned to a shift with all the strong nurses. The unit's best people. And I was immediately aware that I was the weak link. Everything they did was faster. Cleaner. More efficient. They caught things before they became problems. They had rapport with patients that I work for. They moved with purpose while I was thinking through my decisions. And by midshift I was in my head about my incompetence. I was comparing every interaction I had to how they'd handled a similar situation. And I was losing every comparison. A patient asked a question and I was thoughtful about answering it. The nurse next to me answered the same type of question with immediate clarity. Same information. Different delivery. Hers seemed better. I started to think that maybe I shouldn't be doing this. That maybe I should be in a different role. That maybe I'd made a mistake becoming a nurse. Because I'm clearly not as good at it as the people around me. And the spiral started. Comparison to comparison. Moment to moment. By the end of the shift I felt like I'd been exposed. Like everyone there knew what I was beginning to suspect. That I'm not actually qualified. That I'm skating by on luck and not on competence. That someday someone's going to call me out for being a fraud.

## STORY v05
---
path: the mirror of other people's confidence
BAND: 5
SEMANTIC_FAMILY: SF5
---
I've internalized the belief that the other nurses are actually competent and I'm just pretending. That they have something I don't have. That if I compare myself to them long enough I'll eventually understand what it is I'm missing. But the comparison is broken. I'm comparing my internal reality to their external performance. I know my thoughts. I know my doubts. I know my uncertainty. I don't know theirs. I'm only seeing what they show me. And what they show me looks like confidence because I'm assuming that confidence equals competence. But maybe they're also going home and questioning themselves. Maybe they're also wondering if they're good enough. Maybe they're also comparing themselves to someone else and losing. But I can't see that. I only see the performance. And my performance looks worse. So I've started to believe that they're actually better and I'm actually worse. And that belief is starting to affect how I work. I'm more hesitant. I'm more likely to defer to their opinions. I'm less likely to trust my own clinical judgment. And in doing that I'm actually becoming worse. The comparison has become a self-fulfilling prophecy. I believe I'm less competent so I act less competent so I become less competent. And somewhere in there I lost the ability to tell the difference between what I actually am and what I think I should be.
