## STORY v01
---
path: the mistake you can't forget
BAND: 1
SEMANTIC_FAMILY: SF1
---
I gave a medication once with the wrong concentration. It was caught before it made it to the patient. My preceptor found it when she was double-checking my work. The medication was still in the syringe. Nothing bad happened. But I was exposed. My preceptor knew I'd made an error. The pharmacist knew. And I knew. And for months after I was scared that someone was going to figure out that I wasn't careful enough. That I was careless. That I was the type of nurse who makes mistakes. A preventable error. The thing they teach you never to do. And I'd done it. And everyone knew. The shame of that was immense. I kept thinking about it. I kept replaying how it happened. I kept thinking about what could have happened if my preceptor hadn't caught it. And I became convinced that the only reason I hadn't killed someone was luck. That I'm not actually competent enough to be alone with patients. That I'm holding on by the skin of my teeth and waiting for the day when my incompetence is finally exposed. That error made me believe that I'm not meant to do this. That I slipped through nursing school somehow. That I'm a fraud waiting to be discovered.

## STORY v02
---
path: the clinical judgment you don't trust
BAND: 2
SEMANTIC_FAMILY: SF2
---
I didn't call the doctor about a change I noticed in a patient. It was subtle. It was something you had to be watching for. I was watching for it because I'd been burned before. But I wasn't certain enough to call the attending. I wasn't sure my observation was clinically significant. So I waited. I watched the patient more closely. And an hour later the patient decompensated. The change I'd noticed was the beginning of the decompensation. And now the patient's outcome might be worse because I didn't have confidence in my clinical judgment. Because I didn't trust myself enough to call it in. And the shame is this: I knew something was wrong. My nursing instinct was correct. But I didn't trust it because I don't believe in myself. Because I think I'm not actually competent enough to have clinical judgment that matters. And that belief cost something. And now I'm carrying it. The attending didn't blame me. No one blamed me. But I blamed myself. Because I knew. And I didn't act on it. And that's the shameful part. That my doubt in myself might have contributed to patient harm. That my imposter syndrome is no longer just about my own insecurity. It's about the patients who might suffer from my lack of confidence.

## STORY v03
---
path: the credential that feels borrowed
BAND: 3
SEMANTIC_FAMILY: SF3
---
I have an RN license. It's laminated. It has my name on it. It says I'm qualified to practice nursing independently. And I look at it and I think about how I got it. I think about the NCLEX. I think about how I passed it. I passed it, which means I met the minimum standard of knowledge. But passing a test doesn't mean I'm actually competent. It means I answered enough questions correctly. It doesn't mean I'm a good nurse. It doesn't mean I have sound clinical judgment. It doesn't mean I deserve to be making decisions about people's medical care. I have the credential. But I didn't earn it in the way that good nurses earn it. Good nurses have inherent knowledge. They just know things. They pick it up. They absorb it. And they perform with confidence. I'm here still thinking through everything. Still questioning every decision. Still feeling like I'm pretending to be a nurse. And one day someone's going to look at my license and look at my actual performance and realize that there's been a mistake. That I shouldn't have passed the NCLEX. That I shouldn't have this license. That I'm a fraud. And the shame of that is that I think they'd be right. I feel like a fraud. I feel like I borrowed someone else's credential and I'm wearing it like a costume.

## STORY v04
---
path: the day you felt truly exposed
BAND: 4
SEMANTIC_FAMILY: SF4
---
I was standing in the break room and two nurses were talking about a patient. A patient that I'd been caring for. And they were discussing her clinical course with a depth of understanding that made it clear that they'd really thought about it. They understood the pathophysiology. They understood the decision-making. And I realized in that moment that I'd just been following orders. I didn't actually understand the case. I was managing tasks related to the case but I wasn't thinking critically about it. I wasn't engaging with it clinically the way they were. And I thought about how if they'd asked me about that patient in front of an attending I would have stumbled through an answer. I would have provided the basics but I wouldn't have had the depth of understanding that makes someone sound competent. And the shame hit me. That I'm out here practicing nursing without really understanding what I'm doing. That I'm going through the motions. That my patients are trusting me with their care and I'm not actually equipped to provide it at the level that a real nurse would. I'm just managing the surface-level tasks. I'm checking boxes. And one of these days someone's going to realize that. Someone's going to test my knowledge and discover that I don't actually have any.

## STORY v05
---
path: the shame that becomes self-fulfilling
BAND: 5
SEMANTIC_FAMILY: SF5
---
The shame of imposter syndrome is that it changes how I show up. I come to work already convinced that I'm not good enough. I'm already in a state of shame before anything happens. And that shame makes me smaller. It makes me less likely to speak up. It makes me less likely to trust my judgment. It makes me more likely to defer to others. And in doing that I actually become less competent. I stop learning because I'm convinced I can't understand. I stop trying because I'm convinced I'll fail. I stop risking because I'm convinced I'll be exposed. And in that paralysis I'm no longer developing as a nurse. I'm just getting older while staying the same. And the shame ensures that I never actually move past the imposter feeling because I stop doing the things that would build real competence. The shame is a self-fulfilling prophecy. I believe I'm a fraud so I act like a fraud so I become a fraud. And there's no way out of it because the shame keeps me from doing the very things that would prove I'm not a fraud. I'm locked in a cycle where my shame prevents me from gaining the competence that would dissolve the shame. And I don't know how to break it. I don't know if I can.
