# millennial_women_professionals × imposter_syndrome × waiting to be found out
# Slot type: EXERCISE
# Count: 30
# Notes: step-based (Step 1/2/3)
---
## EXERCISE v01
Step 1: List three accomplishments from the past year. Not the ones you're proud of. The ones you actually did.

Step 2: For each one, write down how you did it. Not why you don't deserve credit. How you did it. What decisions did you make?

Step 3: Read it back. Notice the knowledge, the judgment, the skill embedded in each one. This isn't luck. This is you.
---
## EXERCISE v02
Step 1: Identify the accomplishment that didn't land for you. The one that feels like it shouldn't count.

Step 2: Write down every small decision that led to it. Trace the line of your competence. You made ten decisions that got you here.

Step 3: Imagine explaining this to someone else. How would you tell this story? Most of us tell ourselves a luck story we'd never tell anyone else.
---
## EXERCISE v03
Step 1: Write down your internal evidence for being an imposter. The specific reasons you don't belong.

Step 2: For each one, ask: Where did I get this standard? Am I applying it to myself that I apply to others?

Step 3: Rewrite each one as a data point instead of a verdict. "I'm still learning X" is different from "I don't belong.".
---
## EXERCISE v04
Step 1: Before the next meeting where you feel like an imposter, list what you actually know about the topic.

Step 2: List what you don't know. Be specific. Not "everything." The actual gaps.

Step 3: Go to the meeting knowing both. You're not supposed to know everything. The gap between knowing and not knowing is expertise.
---
## EXERCISE v05
Step 1: Identify one person you deeply respect. Write down three things you know they've gotten wrong or learned the hard way.

Step 2: Would those mistakes disqualify them? Or are they part of how they learned?

Step 3: Apply that standard to yourself. Your learning curve isn't evidence of fraud. It's evidence of growth.
---
## EXERCISE v06
Step 1: Write down the specific moment when the doubt arrived. What changed? The title? The responsibility?

Step 2: Notice: Is the doubt about your actual capability, or about the transition into a new role?

Step 3: Separate the doubt from the competence. You might be uncertain about the new role and still completely capable of doing it.
