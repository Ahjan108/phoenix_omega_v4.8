## COMPRESSION v01
compression_family: C1
Sleep anxiety is not primarily a sleep problem. It's a nervous system problem. Your system is operating in threat-detection mode when it should be operating in rest mode. No amount of sleep hygiene will fix this if you're still in survival activation. The work is shifting your nervous system's baseline during the day, not just managing the night.

---
## COMPRESSION v02
compression_family: C1
Your brain is trying to solve tomorrow by staying awake tonight. This is backwards. You're not solving tomorrow. You're stealing the one resource—rest—that would actually make you more capable tomorrow. The insomnia is self-defeating. Recognizing this is the moment when change is possible.

---
## COMPRESSION v03
compression_family: C2
The anxiety about tomorrow arrives at night because that's when the busy-ness stops and you actually have time to think. The day fills with distraction. The night has none. So the night becomes the place where everything you didn't process emerges. The solution isn't better sleep. It's better processing during the day so there's less to think about at night.

---
## COMPRESSION v04
compression_family: C2
Sleep anxiety is often a symptom of over-functioning during the day. You've been "on" for 14-16 hours. Your nervous system never got a chance to downshift gradually. Bedtime is supposed to be a gentle descent into rest. If you're still in performance mode at bedtime, your system won't recognize the transition. Build in transition time before bed, not just a sharp on/off.

---
## COMPRESSION v05
compression_family: C3
The problem with trying harder to sleep is that trying is incompatible with sleep. Sleep requires a kind of surrender that trying prevents. You have to approach sleep sideways—practice rest, not sleep; accept wakefulness, not fight it; move toward calm, not force relaxation. All of these are the opposite of trying harder.

---
## COMPRESSION v06
compression_family: C3
You cannot think your way into sleep. Thinking is what's keeping you awake. At night, your job is not to solve problems or think clearly. Your job is to not think. This is hard for someone whose mind is usually productive. But sleep requires unproductivity. That's the bargain.

## COMPRESSION v07
compression_family: C4
Your sleep anxiety is stored in your body as much as your mind. You carry the tension of your worries in your nervous system. Releasing sleep anxiety requires embodied practices—breathing, movement, sensation—not just mental strategies. Work with your body, not against it.

---
## COMPRESSION v08
compression_family: C4
The more you blame yourself for the sleep anxiety, the more anxious you become. Self-judgment is another form of activation. Compassion for yourself—"My nervous system is trying to protect me. That's its job. I can help it learn that nights are safe"—is more effective than "I should be able to sleep better than this."

## COMPRESSION v09
compression_family: C5
Sleep anxiety is a sign that your daytime is unsustainable. Your system is signaling: "This pace is too much. This load is too heavy. This uncertainty is too much." Listen to the signal. Change the daytime. The nighttime will follow.

## COMPRESSION v10
compression_family: C1
You're lying awake trying to create certainty about tomorrow. But certainty isn't available. Tomorrow is always uncertain. You have to learn to be okay with that—not just intellectually, but in your nervous system. This takes practice. This takes exposure to uncertainty without harm. Sleep anxiety is your system resisting that exposure.

## COMPRESSION v11
compression_family: C2
Most of what you're worried about at night won't happen. Most of it is anticipatory anxiety, not actual threat. But your nervous system can't tell the difference between an actual tiger and a predicted tiger. Both activate threat response. You need to train your system to more accurately assess what's actually threatening versus what's just possible.

---
## COMPRESSION v12
compression_family: C2
Sleep anxiety is partly about loss of control. During the day you can manage things, solve things, influence outcomes. At night, you can't. Sleep is something that happens to you, not something you do. That surrender is what's hard. But it's also what sleep requires. Practice surrender in small ways during the day so it's available at night.

## COMPRESSION v13
compression_family: C3
The loop of anxiety → trying to sleep harder → more anxiety → less sleep is self-reinforcing. Breaking it requires doing the opposite of what the anxiety suggests. Anxiety says: problem-solve. You do: accept. Anxiety says: stay alert. You do: drift. The opposite responses break the loop.

## COMPRESSION v14
compression_family: C3
Your competence during the day is sometimes a liability at night. You're used to solving things through effort and analysis. Sleep doesn't respond to effort. This creates a sense of helplessness that can increase anxiety. You're not helpless. You're just in a domain where effort is counterproductive. That's a different dynamic.

## COMPRESSION v15
compression_family: C4
Sleep anxiety is not a character flaw. It's not something you're doing wrong. It's a predictable nervous system response to a life that feels high-stakes and unpredictable. Most high-functioning people have versions of this. You're not broken. You're just activated.

## COMPRESSION v16
compression_family: C4
The thought that keeps you awake—the one you've thought a hundred times, that you know won't resolve—is a habit. Habits can be interrupted. Not through willpower but through consistent, small redirections. Every time you notice the thought and redirect, you're creating a new groove. Eventually, the new groove is as automatic as the old one.

You're trying to achieve certainty about tomorrow so you can rest. But rest is not conditional on certainty. Rest is a choice you make despite the uncertainty. Once you practice making that choice, sleep often follows. But the choice comes first.

compression_family: C2
The night is when your nervous system's threat-detection is highest relative to your rational mind. You believe catastrophic narratives at 3am that you'd dismiss at 3pm. You're not less intelligent at night. Your threat-detection system is just louder. Learning to not believe everything it tells you is the skill.

compression_family: C3
Most sleep advice addresses sleep mechanics. But sleep anxiety is about nervous system state. You can have perfect sleep hygiene and still be anxious. You can have imperfect hygiene and sleep well if your nervous system is calm. Work upstream, with your system state, not just the mechanics.

The anxiety is real. The threat is not. Both can be true. Your nervous system is genuinely activated. But nothing bad is actually happening. Learning to sit with real anxiety in the absence of real threat is the practice that calms sleep anxiety.

You're trying to do in the night what can only be done in the day. You're trying to build confidence, solve problems, achieve certainty. None of these happen at 2am. They happen during daylight with a clear mind. The night is for rest. Use the day for everything else.
