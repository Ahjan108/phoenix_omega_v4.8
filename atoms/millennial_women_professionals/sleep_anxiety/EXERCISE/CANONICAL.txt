## EXERCISE v01
The Pre-Sleep Brain Dump. Step 1: Before bed, spend 15 minutes writing down every thought, worry, plan, and unfinished thought that's in your head. No organization—just getting it out. Step 2: The goal is to externalize what's cycling. Once it's on paper, your brain doesn't have to hold it. Step 3: When the thought returns at night, remind yourself: "I wrote that down. It's safe. It's captured." Step 4: This separates the actual problems (which exist) from the rumination (which is in your brain). Step 5: You might still think about them at night. But the charge is different when they're externalized.

## EXERCISE v02
The Nervous System Reset. Step 1: Earlier in the evening, before the anxiety spiral, do something that activates your parasympathetic nervous system. This is something gentle, intentional, slow—not a workout. Step 2: Examples: slow walking, gentle stretching, warm shower, sitting outside, listening to music. Step 3: The goal is to land your nervous system in rest-and-digest, not in alert-and-prepare. Step 4: This is not relaxation. It's your nervous system changing its baseline. Step 5: Do this consistently. You're training your body toward calm before bedtime.

## EXERCISE v03
The Anxiety Container. Step 1: In the evening (not at night), identify what's making you anxious about tomorrow. Step 2: Ask yourself: "What can I actually do about this before tomorrow?" Make a list. Step 3: Do those things now, if they're doable. Step 4: For the things you can't do now, create a specific time you'll address them tomorrow. Step 5: Once you've contained the action items, the thought should have less power. You've honored the concern by planning to address it.

## EXERCISE v04
The Thought-Passing Protocol. Step 1: During the night, when anxiety thoughts arrive, imagine they're cars passing on a street. You don't chase them. You don't engage them. You watch them pass. Step 2: You're not trying to stop the thoughts. You're changing your relationship to them. Step 3: When you notice you've grabbed onto a thought (started engaging it), pause and return to watching. Step 4: This is meditation, essentially, but framed as "letting traffic pass" rather than "clearing your mind." Step 5: The traffic will keep coming. Your job is to not climb into the cars.

## EXERCISE v05
The Tomorrow Briefing. Step 1: In the evening, create a very simple plan for tomorrow. Not a detailed plan. A basic outline. Step 2: Make it specific enough that your brain doesn't have to solve it in real-time. "The 10am meeting is about the budget. I've reviewed the numbers. The talking points are in my email draft." Step 3: This gives your nervous system permission to rest because the problem-solving is already done. Step 4: If you think of something else at night, the response is: "I'll add it to tomorrow's briefing." Step 5: You're moving problem-solving into the light where it works. Keeping it at night only activates more anxiety.

## EXERCISE v06
The Body Scan with Action. Step 1: Lie in bed and slowly scan your body, starting at your feet. Step 2: The standard practice is to notice tension and release it. Step 3: But here's the variation: where you notice tension, ask yourself: "What am I worried about right here in my body?" Step 4: Match the tension location to the worry. This creates a mind-body connection. Step 5: Once you've identified the connection, you can work with both—releasing the physical tension and the mental worry simultaneously.

## EXERCISE v07
The Permission to Rise. Step 1: If you're awake at 2am and you know sleep isn't coming, stop fighting it. Step 2: Give yourself permission to get out of bed. Step 3: Go somewhere else. Do something that's slightly engaging but not stimulating. Not your phone. Not work. Maybe journaling, maybe reading something light, maybe sitting quietly. Step 4: The permission to rise actually makes sleep more likely than the effort to force sleep while lying down. Step 5: Once you've genuinely surrendered to wakefulness, sleep often arrives because you've stopped the cycle of trying and failing.

## EXERCISE v08
The Worry Timebox. Step 1: Designate a specific 15-minute period in the late afternoon or early evening as "Worry Time." Step 2: During this time, you're allowed—encouraged—to think about everything you're worried about. Full permission. Step 3: Outside this time, when worries arrive, you say: "I'll think about this during Worry Time." Step 4: This signals to your brain that the concerns are legitimate but contained. Step 5: Often, by the time Worry Time arrives, some of the worries have resolved. The ones that remain get proper attention instead of recurring all night.

## EXERCISE v09
The Sleep as Receiving. Step 1: Sleep is not something you achieve through effort. It's something you receive through surrender. Step 2: Instead of "I need to fall asleep," try: "I'm going to rest and let sleep come if it does." Step 3: The shift from doing to receiving is neurologically significant. It changes your nervous system's baseline. Step 4: If you're lying in bed, try: "I'm resting. Whether I sleep or not, resting is the actual goal." Step 5: This removes the pass/fail evaluation that creates performance pressure around sleep.

## EXERCISE v10
The Time-Release Thought. Step 1: When an anxious thought arrives at night, write it down if you can (without fully waking up—a notepad by the bed). Step 2: Or, if you can't write it down, imagine putting it in a container. "I'm putting this thought in the blue box. I'll address it tomorrow." Step 3: The externalization gives your mind permission to release it. It's captured. It won't be lost. Step 4: Your job at night is not to solve problems. It's to note them and defer them. Step 5: This is why the pre-sleep brain dump works—you've already done the deferring.

## EXERCISE v11
The Exhaustion Inventory. Step 1: If you're lying awake, make a list of everything you've done in the last 24 hours. Step 2: See the fullness of it. You've done a lot. Step 3: Ask yourself: "Did I actually have the bandwidth to solve the things I'm worried about?" Step 4: Usually the answer is: "I was already full." Step 5: The exhaustion is real. The additional problem-solving at night is not something your system can actually handle. Acknowledging that might reduce the urgency of trying.

## EXERCISE v12
The Anxiety Audit. Step 1: In the evening, ask yourself: "What am I actually anxious about?" Make a list. Step 2: For each item, ask: "Is this something I control? Can I do something about it today?" Step 3: If yes, do it. If no, write: "This is outside my control." Step 4: The things outside your control don't need your nighttime thinking. They need acceptance. Step 5: Separating what you control from what you don't is clarifying. It reduces the sense of urgency around the uncontrollables.

## EXERCISE v13
The Bedtime Ritual Reset. Step 1: Create a very specific, very consistent bedtime ritual. The repetition is what matters, not the content. Step 2: It could be: wash face, journal three sentences, read five pages, lights off. Step 3: The ritual signals to your nervous system: "This is the transition. Sleep is coming." Step 4: Consistency is key. Your body needs to know what to expect. Step 5: If the ritual includes something calming (not exciting, not work), that's the neurological signal to shift toward rest.

## EXERCISE v14
The Future Scaffolding. Step 1: If you're anxious about tomorrow, create a specific, detailed plan for how you'll handle your worst-case scenario. Step 2: Not "I hope it doesn't go wrong." But "If it goes wrong, here's what I'll do." Step 3: This is not catastrophizing. This is planning. Step 4: Once you have a plan for the worst case, your nervous system can stand down. There's a protocol. You're prepared. Step 5: This takes maybe 10 minutes of planning but saves you hours of nighttime worry.

## EXERCISE v15
The Sleep Deprivation Acceptance. Step 1: Some nights won't have good sleep. This is true. Step 2: Instead of fighting this truth, make a plan: "If I don't sleep tonight, here's how I'll function tomorrow." Step 3: You'll drink water. You'll move your body. You'll break tasks into smaller pieces. Step 4: You have survived sleepless nights before. You know how to function. Step 5: Acceptance sometimes paradoxically makes sleep more likely, but even if it doesn't, you're no longer fighting against the inevitable.

## EXERCISE v16
The Nervous System Temperature Check. Step 1: Before bed, check your nervous system status. On a scale of 1-10, how activated are you? Step 2: If you're above a 5, you're not in a sleep-compatible state. Step 3: Spend 10-15 minutes bringing the temperature down: slower breathing, slower movement, dimmer lights, quieter environment. Step 4: Aim for a 4 or below—calm but alert. Step 5: This check and adjustment become a nightly calibration that preps your system for rest.

## EXERCISE v17
The Thought Parking Lot. Step 1: Keep a notebook by your bed. Step 2: Any thought that arrives during the night gets written there. Not fully. Just a note: "Email about budget" or "Ask about timeline." Step 3: The act of writing it down tells your brain: "This is captured. I don't need to remember it. I don't need to solve it now." Step 4: In the morning, these notes become your priority list, but at night, they're just parked. Step 5: The parking lot reduces the amount of mental RAM your brain needs to use.

## EXERCISE v18
The Catastrophe Chain Check. Step 1: When you're lying awake worried about something, trace the chain: "If this happens, then this, then this." Step 2: Usually by step three or four, the chain reaches something that's actually survivable. Step 3: Once you see you could survive the end of the chain, the urgency of preventing the beginning diminishes. Step 4: You're not trying to prevent bad things from happening. You're trying to see that you're not helpless if they do. Step 5: This reframes the nighttime worry as preparation for manageable outcomes, not prevention of unmanageable ones.

## EXERCISE v19
The Sensory Grounding. Step 1: If anxiety is running high at night, shift from the mental (which is spinning) to the sensory (which is grounding). Step 2: Notice five things you can see (even in the dark, you can see something). Four things you can feel (texture of sheets, coolness of pillow). Three things you can hear. Two things you can smell. One thing you can taste. Step 3: This 5-4-3-2-1 practice brings you into your body and the present moment. Step 4: It's hard to be anxious about the future while you're present-sensing. Step 5: Practice this during the day so it's available automatically at night.

## EXERCISE v20
The Evening Energy Inventory. Step 1: Around 4-5pm, ask yourself: "How much mental energy do I have left for problem-solving today?" Step 2: Be honest. If the answer is "none" or "very little," your work is done. You're not doing additional thinking about unresolved things. Step 3: Anything unresolved gets a time stamp: "I'll think about this tomorrow at 10am when I have energy." Step 4: You're protecting your evening and nighttime from the demands of problem-solving that you don't have capacity for. Step 5: This is not avoidance. It's resource management.

## EXERCISE v21
The Wake-Time Acceptance. Step 1: Instead of "How do I fall asleep?" ask "If I'm awake, what's the best thing I can do with this time?" Step 2: Give yourself permission to just rest. Not sleep. Rest. Step 3: Lying quietly in the dark, not doing anything, is actually still valuable for your body. It's not as good as sleep, but it's not zero. Step 4: When you remove the goal of sleep and replace it with "I'm resting," the pressure drops. Step 5: Sometimes removing the goal paradoxically achieves the goal.

## EXERCISE v22
The Daytime Practice Foundation. Step 1: Sleep anxiety at night often reflects low tolerance for uncertainty during the day. Step 2: During the day, practice making decisions with incomplete information. Practice moving forward despite doubt. Step 3: These are the tolerance muscles. Build them during the day. Step 4: At night, you'll have more tolerance for ambiguity and unsolved problems. Step 5: The nighttime sleep is less about sleep hygiene and more about daytime resilience training.

## EXERCISE v23
The Threat Assessment Reality Check. Step 1: When you're anxious at night, ask yourself: "Am I actually in danger right now?" Step 2: The answer is almost always no. You're safe. The threat is imagined. Step 3: Say it: "I am safe right now. The danger is imagined." Step 4: This is not about positive thinking. It's about accurate threat assessment. Your nervous system has been overestimating danger. Step 5: Correct threat assessment often corrects the nervous system's response.

## EXERCISE v24
The Exhaustion-as-Data Framework. Step 1: Pay attention to what time of day your sleep anxiety is worst. Step 2: Is it on nights after high-stress days? After days with poor boundaries? After days of non-stop activity? Step 3: The pattern reveals something about what's creating the anxiety. Step 4: Once you see the pattern, you can work upstream—managing stress levels, setting boundaries, building in recovery. Step 5: You're not treating sleep anxiety as a sleep problem. You're treating it as a daytime management problem.

## EXERCISE v25
The Calm Anchor. Step 1: Identify a moment or image where you felt genuinely calm. Step 2: Before bed, spend a few moments there in memory. What did it feel like in your body? What were you doing? Step 3: As you're lying in bed, return to that anchored feeling. Not visualizing—actually feeling. Step 4: Your nervous system can recognize calm as a state it knows how to do. Step 5: This is different from trying to create calm. You're returning to something your system already knows.

## EXERCISE v26
The Story Change. Step 1: The story your brain tells at night is usually: "I won't sleep, I'll be terrible tomorrow, everything will fall apart." Step 2: Create an alternative story: "I might sleep. If I don't, I'm resilient enough to function. My work will be fine." Step 3: The alternative story is not more true. But it's not less true either. Both are possible. Step 4: You're not forcing optimism. You're offering your brain an equally plausible alternative narrative. Step 5: Over time, your brain will access both stories. This expands what feels possible.

## EXERCISE v27
The Nervous System Coaching. Step 1: Talk to your nervous system like you'd talk to someone you care about. Step 2: "I know you're worried about tomorrow. I've got this. You can rest now. Nothing needs to be solved tonight." Step 3: This seems silly but it works because you're acknowledging what your nervous system is trying to do (protect you) while redirecting it. Step 4: You're not fighting your system. You're coaching it. Step 5: Over time, your system learns that night is safe and rest is possible.

## EXERCISE v28
The Sleep Debt Perspective. Step 1: View sleep not as a nightly task but as a weekly rhythm. Step 2: Some nights might be poor. Others might be excellent. Over the week, your system is getting sleep. Step 3: This removes the pressure from tonight. One night doesn't determine the week. Step 4: You're in a rhythm, not in a daily pass-fail system. Step 5: This shift in perspective often reduces nighttime anxiety because the stakes are smaller.

---
## EXERCISE v29
The Cognitive Load Reduction. Step 1: Everything you haven't written down is taking up mental RAM. Step 2: Before bed, externalize everything: to-do lists, plans, worries, ideas. Step 3: Once it's external, your brain can release it. Step 4: Test this: the nights when you've done a thorough brain dump, sleep is often better. Step 5: It's not magic. It's just the cognitive load difference.

---
## EXERCISE v30
The Acceptance Practice. Step 1: The core of sleep anxiety is usually the rejection of how things are: "I shouldn't be anxious. I should sleep. I should be better at this." Step 2: Practice accepting: "Right now, I'm anxious. Right now, I'm not sleeping. And that's what's happening." Step 3: Acceptance is not resignation. It's actually the gateway to change. Step 4: When you stop fighting what is, your nervous system can actually shift. Step 5: The paradox of sleep anxiety is that acceptance often leads to sleep, while rejection perpetuates wakefulness.
