# millennial_women_professionals × social_anxiety × the performance that never turns off
# Slot type: EXERCISE
# Count: 30
# Notes: step-based (Step 1/2/3)
---
## EXERCISE v01
Step 1: Before you enter a social situation, pause for 10 seconds. Notice where the anxiety lives in your body. Name it: "This is what anxiety feels like right now."

Step 2: Ground yourself in three concrete facts about the space: the temperature, the light, the number of people. Say these internally. These are real. You are real.

Step 3: Make one deliberate choice for the first two minutes: you will either ask one question or listen without performing. Choose one. This removes the pressure to be perfect.
---
## EXERCISE v02
Step 1: After the conversation, write down one thing you said that you're replaying. Just one sentence.

Step 2: Ask yourself: Would I judge someone else for saying this? Be honest. Most of us are much harsher with ourselves.

Step 3: If you would judge someone else, ask why. What are you afraid this sentence reveals about you? Challenge it with evidence.
---
## EXERCISE v03
Step 1: Identify the social situation you're dreading. Write down the worst-case scenario your anxiety predicts.

Step 2: Write down what you actually know will happen based on past experience. List three times this went fine.

Step 3: Before you go, imagine yourself in the room. Not succeeding perfectly. Just being present. The anxiety might stay. You're separating it from the outcome.
---
## EXERCISE v04
Step 1: Notice when you're replaying something you said wrong. The moment you catch yourself, pause.

Step 2: Ask: Did the conversation end? Do the people still respect you? Am I the only one who remembers this moment?

Step 3: Replace the replay with a memory of a time you felt easy in a social moment. Not because you said something perfect. Because you felt safe. Rest there for 30 seconds.
---
## EXERCISE v05
Step 1: Before a social situation, commit to one small authenticity: you will say one thing that feels true instead of performed.

Step 2: In the situation, when you notice yourself performing, pause. Ask: What do I actually think? Say that instead.

Step 3: Afterward, notice: Did being slightly more real make things worse or better? Most of us find connection instead of rejection.
---
## EXERCISE v06
Step 1: List the specific fears about the social situation. Not general ("I'll be awkward"). Specific ("I'll say something stupid and they'll laugh").

Step 2: For each fear, ask: Is this based on what's actually happened before, or on what I'm afraid might happen?

Step 3: Counter each fear with actual evidence from past situations where you were nervous and it was fine anyway.
