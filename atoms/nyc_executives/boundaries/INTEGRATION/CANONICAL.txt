

## INTEGRATION v01
---
mode: BODY_LANDED
reframe_type: BODY_FACT
weight: standard
carry_line: "Your spine knows constant yes-saying."
---
The text comes in at 11:47 PM. Your body contracts before you even read it. The chest tightens. The mind immediately calculates whether you can respond before sleep. This is not a response to the content. This is a response to the pattern: the expectation of reachability has become a physical fact. Your nervous system has learned that availability equals your market value. It has learned that boundaries feel like rejection. The body holds this belief in the shoulders, the jaw, the perpetual low-grade vigilance. The moment you recognize the contraction, you've found the pattern. The tightness is the cost of the yes. The signal is already there, written in your nervous system, long before the actual work arrives.

## INTEGRATION v02
---
mode: COST_VISIBLE
reframe_type: COST_UNCOUNTED
weight: standard
carry_line: "Nobody measures what always-reachable costs."
---
The spreadsheet doesn't show it. The hours logged don't show it. The billable work doesn't show it. What shows up instead: the client who texts on weekends because that's the pattern you've established. The team member who interrupts your vacation because they learned you'll answer. The mental space that never actually closes. The dinner table conversation that never fully lands because part of your attention is listening for the phone. The cost of being the person everyone can call is the cost of never being fully unavailable. This cost appears as fatigue that doesn't match the actual hours worked. It appears as the sense that you can never actually rest. It accumulates as a low-level resentment toward the people who've come to depend on your constant availability. The organization benefits. You pay.
---

## INTEGRATION v03
---
mode: STILL_HERE
reframe_type: TIME_SHIFT
weight: light
carry_line: "Still here. You survived the fear of no."
note: STILL-HERE mode — final chapter only, one per book
---
You remember the first time you said it. No. To a client request that you didn't have bandwidth for. To a meeting that didn't serve your actual priorities. To the expectation of weekend work on a project that wasn't actually urgent. The fear was enormous. The fear was that saying no meant you were unreliable. That it meant you were leaving money on the table. That it meant someone more available would take your place. None of that happened. What happened instead: the relationships that mattered remained intact. The clients who needed someone more available found someone. The firm continued. You discovered that your market value wasn't built entirely on your relentless accessibility. You're still here. The firms that tried to replace your availability learned they couldn't. The clients who valued you valued you for more than your willingness to work weekends. The fear about no was larger than the actual consequences.

## INTEGRATION v04
---
mode: QUESTION_OPEN
reframe_type: QUESTION_REFRAME
weight: standard
carry_line: "Is the boundary about others or about you."
---
The question isn't whether they'll respect your boundary if you set it. They probably won't, at first. They've trained themselves to access you without limits. The question is whether you can respect your own boundary. Whether you can hold the line when the discomfort arrives. When the email sits unanswered at 8PM and the anxiety fires because someone might need something. The question isn't whether your firm will continue without your constant availability. It will. The question is what you're willing to tolerate in your own nervous system in exchange for the security of making yourself indispensable.
---

## INTEGRATION v05
---
mode: SOMEONE_ELSE
reframe_type: OTHER_PERSON
weight: heavy
carry_line: "Your peer is learning the cost."
---
You watch her manage three children, two divorces, and a major practice area. You watch her answer emails between school pickup and 10 PM calls. You watch her body change shape—the shoulders tighter, the face more drawn. You watch her stay yes to everything because saying no feels dangerous. She's running the same calculus you ran five years ago. The same belief that boundaries equal vulnerability. The same fear that the moment she becomes unavailable, the whole structure collapses. It doesn't collapse. It just requires her to pay the cost in her body instead.

## INTEGRATION v06
---
mode: BODY_LANDED
reframe_type: BODY_FACT
weight: interrupt_landing
carry_line: "The spine releases when you say no."
note: interrupt_landing — use after The Interrupt story, quietest atom in the book
---
You decline the meeting. You don't respond to the 9 PM text. You close the laptop at six. The discomfort is immediate. The guilt is immediate. The fear that something will collapse is immediate. And underneath it: the body begins to land. The shoulders drop. The breath deepens. The constant listening stops. This is what it feels like when you become your own priority.

## INTEGRATION v07
---
mode: COST_VISIBLE
reframe_type: COST_UNCOUNTED
weight: heavy
carry_line: "The cost of yes compounds year by year."
---
You've been saying yes for fifteen years. The cost is paid in the relationships that never grew because you didn't have presence. In the health that eroded because rest never happened. In the creativity that atrophied because your mental space was always occupied. In the self that you'd recognize if you ever stopped working long enough to find it. This cost doesn't appear on any document. It doesn't reduce your partnership percentage. It doesn't get named in exit interviews. It appears in the mirror. In the exhaustion that no amount of vacation fixes. In the sense that your life is something that happens to you between work commitments.
