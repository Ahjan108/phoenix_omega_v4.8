Marcus sits in the morning meeting, surrounded by eight colleagues. They're discussing Q3 targets. His targets are down. Not catastrophically—still respectable compared to industry standard. But down from his Q2. His face warms. Not from embarrassment exactly. From something deeper. A feeling of being seen. Everyone knows his numbers are visible. Everyone knows. His chest tightens. His shoulders rise slightly. He's brought less this quarter. In his mind, that translates to: he is less this quarter. His ribs narrow. The gap between what he projected and what he delivered is now public. And in that gap, something is missing: the version of himself he presented. That absence feels like exposure. Like he's been caught.
