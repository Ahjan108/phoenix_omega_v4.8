# tech_finance_burnout × burnout × the system running past empty
# Slot type: EXERCISE
# Count: 30

---
## EXERCISE v01
Stop debugging yourself like you're a system. Step 1: List all the ways you're trying to optimize your own functioning. Step 2: These are patches. Step 3: You're not a system to optimize. You're a person who needs rest. That's different.

---
## EXERCISE v02
Map what you're spending resources on that doesn't matter. Step 1: What meetings could be emails? What tasks are busywork? What communication is noise? Step 2: Calculate the hours. Step 3: Identify what you could say no to. The answer is: more than you think.

---
## EXERCISE v03
Name the lie you're living. Step 1: What are you telling yourself about why this pace is necessary? Step 2: Is it true or is it the system's voice in your head? Step 3: Usually it's the latter. See it clearly.

---
## EXERCISE v04
Take actual downtime without productivity. Step 1: One evening, no laptop, no work, no "catching up." Step 2: Experience the anxiety. Step 3: The anxiety is real. The necessity you're convinced of—usually less real.

---
## EXERCISE v05
Calculate your actual capacity vs. demand. Step 1: List what's being asked of you. Step 2: What can you actually deliver? Step 3: The gap is the structural problem. It's not your fault. It's the system's fault.

---
## EXERCISE v06
Audit your metrics addiction. Step 1: What numbers are you tracking obsessively? Step 2: Do they actually tell you what you need to know? Step 3: Usually they're anxiety disguised as measurement. Stop measuring. Start observing.

---
## EXERCISE v07
Set a boundary and hold it. Step 1: Pick something that actually needs to stop (emails after 8 PM, weekend work, something). Step 2: Stop it completely. Step 3: The system won't collapse. You might be shocked how little actually requires your always-on status.

---
## EXERCISE v08
Notice when you're performing competence vs. actually being competent. Step 1: What percentage of your energy goes to appearing okay vs. actually being okay? Step 2: Usually it's 70/30 at least. Step 3: That performance is exhausting. Stop it.

---
## EXERCISE v09
Identify one thing you're doing that you actually don't need to do. Step 1: What's on your plate that would be fine if you stepped back? Step 2: You probably can't name it because everything feels essential. Step 3: Nothing is essential. Propose stepping back from something.

---
## EXERCISE v10
Track your cognitive decline. Step 1: For one week, note when you make mistakes, when you're slow, when you can't focus. Step 2: Is this new or is this baseline? Step 3: If it's new, you're declining. That's data that matters.

---
## EXERCISE v11
Ask: who does this system serve? Step 1: Your constantly being on—does it serve you? Step 2: Does it serve your employer/clients? Probably yes. Step 3: You're serving something that's not serving you back. That's the asymmetry worth naming.

---
## EXERCISE v12
Write what normal would feel like. Step 1: Not theoretically. What would a sustainable pace actually look like for this role? Step 2: Is that available? Step 3: If not, the job is the problem, not you.

---
## EXERCISE v13
Stop treating problems like they're your personal responsibility. Step 1: What problem are you carrying that someone else assigned to you? Step 2: Can it be released back to them? Step 3: Usually yes, but you've internalized it as yours.

---
## EXERCISE v14
Notice the background processing never stops. Step 1: Your mind is always working, always optimizing. Step 2: That's not productivity. That's addiction to productivity. Step 3: Break it. Actually rest. Not productive rest. Just rest.

---
## EXERCISE v15
Identify your escape fantasy. Step 1: What do you dream about doing instead? Step 2: Why aren't you doing it? Step 3: Usually the barrier is financial or identity-based. See what it actually is.

---
## EXERCISE v16
Calculate the cost of staying. Step 1: What has this pace cost you? Health, relationships, joy, sleep. Step 2: Put a time value on it. Step 3: Now ask: is the paycheck worth this cost? Usually no.

---
## EXERCISE v17
Stop solving problems that aren't yours to solve. Step 1: What are you worrying about that someone else should handle? Step 2: Can you release it? Step 3: You're carrying extra weight that isn't yours.

---
## EXERCISE v18
Notice when you start working mentally outside working hours. Step 1: Track it. Step 2: When you catch it, interrupt it. Step 3: This is where the boundary actually lives. Not at 5 PM. In your mind.

---
## EXERCISE v19
Create a real shutdown ritual. Step 1: Design what completely leaving work looks like. Step 2: No checking, no thinking, no solving. Step 3: If you can't actually leave your mind, that's the work. That's what needs to shift.

---
## EXERCISE v20
Ask what would happen if you let something fail. Step 1: What's the one thing you're holding together that could actually break? Step 2: What would happen? Step 3: Usually: you'd fix it, or it would matter less than you think. That experiment is clarifying.

---
## EXERCISE v21
Name one task you do that nobody's actually asked you to do. Step 1: It's probably invisible labor. Something assumed. Step 2: Can you stop doing it? Step 3: Usually yes. You've just never proposed stopping.

---
## EXERCISE v22
Notice the feedback loop of stress-optimization-more-stress. Step 1: You get stressed, optimize harder, which creates more stress. Step 2: Can you interrupt this loop? Step 3: Usually the interruption is: accept the stress and don't optimize.

---
## EXERCISE v23
Define what "enough work" actually means. Step 1: You probably don't have a definition. It's infinite. Step 2: Finite the infinit. Give yourself a number. Step 3: You're practicing: you can set limits, and the world continues.

---
## EXERCISE v24
Write the resignation email you'll never send. Step 1: Say everything you'd say if the outcome didn't matter. Step 2: Don't send it. Step 3: The fact that you needed to write it is the message. What's it telling you?

---
## EXERCISE v25
Stop being the person who knows everything. Step 1: What could you not know? Step 2: Practice saying "I don't know" without immediately solving. Step 3: You don't have to have all the answers. That expectation is the burden.

---
## EXERCISE v26
Notice when you're performing for invisible judges. Step 1: Who are you trying to impress? Step 2: Are they actually watching? Step 3: Usually not. You're maintaining performance for an audience that doesn't exist.

---
## EXERCISE v27
Create a realistic capacity map. Step 1: What can you actually deliver in 40 hours/week? Step 2: Compare to what's being asked. Step 3: The discrepancy is structural. It's not you. It's the ask.

---
## EXERCISE v28
Stop the constant self-monitoring. Step 1: Notice how often you check your own performance. Step 2: That self-surveillance is part of the exhaustion. Step 3: Release the surveillance. Trust yourself without proof.

---
## EXERCISE v29
Identify the version of you that's being erased. Step 1: Who were you before this pace? Step 2: What's gone? Step 3: That loss is real. Grieving it is part of the work.

---
## EXERCISE v30
Do the math on staying vs. leaving. Step 1: What are the financial constraints? Step 2: What are the identity constraints? Step 3: Which one is actually keeping you? That answer matters.
