# tech_finance_burnout × financial_anxiety × the number that never feels safe
# Slot type: EXERCISE
# Count: 30

---
## EXERCISE v01
Separate the analytical from the anxious. Step 1: List facts about your actual financial situation (income, expenses, savings, runway). Step 2: List fears (catastrophe, job loss, market collapse). Step 3: Notice: different categories. The facts are usually solid. The fears are usually untethered to fact. Do the work with facts, not fears.

---
## EXERCISE v02
Stop analyzing and start feeling. Step 1: Notice what emotion comes up when you think about money. Step 2: Don't analyze it, just feel it. Step 3: That feeling is the real data. Analyzing it away just prevents healing.

---
## EXERCISE v03
Name what the money anxiety is actually protecting. Step 1: What bad thing is the anxiety preventing from happening? Step 2: How realistic is that bad thing? Step 3: Usually the anxiety is protecting against something that either won't happen or wouldn't be as catastrophic as feared. Question the protection.

---
## EXERCISE v04
Take a break from monitoring. Step 1: Choose 48 hours where you don't check accounts, news, projections. Step 2: Notice the anxiety that arises. Step 3: Usually nothing bad happens. The monitoring isn't actually protective. It just feels like it is.

---
## EXERCISE v05
Do the financial math once, thoroughly. Step 1: Calculate: what do you need, what do you earn, what's the actual surplus/deficit? Step 2: Put a stake in the ground: this is what's true. Step 3: Refer back to this when anxiety spikes. The anxiety wants you to doubt. The math is the truth.

---
## EXERCISE v06
Ask what belief the anxiety is protecting. Step 1: The anxiety says: the money will run out. Step 2: Ask deeper: if it does, what happens? Step 3: Usually underneath is: you won't survive, you won't be cared for, you'll be alone. That belief is what needs examining, not the numbers.

---
## EXERCISE v07
Stop modeling worst-case scenarios. Step 1: Notice how often you do this. Step 2: For one week, when you catch yourself, replace it with best-case or most-likely-case. Step 3: Your brain is pattern-matching on worst-case because something taught it that. Practice different patterns.

---
## EXERCISE v08
Calculate the cost of the anxiety itself. Step 1: How much time do you spend on financial worry/monitoring? Step 2: Put a time value on it. Step 3: The anxiety is costing you more than an actual financial problem would cost. That's worth acknowledging.

---
## EXERCISE v09
Define "enough" numerically. Step 1: What number would actually feel safe? Step 2: Be specific. Not "security," a number. Step 3: Have you had that number before? Usually: yes. And it didn't quiet the anxiety. The anxiety problem isn't numerical.

---
## EXERCISE v10
Notice the pattern of reassurance-seeking. Step 1: How often do you check accounts looking for reassurance? Step 2: Does the reassurance last? Step 3: Usually it lasts minutes. That temporary relief is keeping you hooked. You need a different kind of reassurance—internal, not external.

---
## EXERCISE v11
Try being bad at tracking one thing. Step 1: Pick something small (coffee spending, transport costs). Step 2: Don't track it for a month. Step 3: You'll likely find: you're fine. The tracking wasn't protective. It was anxiety management that didn't actually manage anything.

---
## EXERCISE v12
Challenge the catastrophe story specifically. Step 1: Write out the worst financial scenario you fear. Step 2: For each catastrophe, ask: could I handle this? Do I have resources? Step 3: Usually you'll find: you're more resilient than the anxiety suggests.

---
## EXERCISE v13
Notice when you're using analysis as avoidance. Step 1: Are you reading about money because you need information, or because the activity feels safe? Step 2: Probably the second. Step 3: The reading makes you feel productive about the anxiety. But it's not solving the anxiety. It's feeding it.

---
## EXERCISE v14
Audit your financial decisions for anxiety. Step 1: List recent money decisions. Step 2: For each, ask: was this based on actual need or anxiety? Step 3: You'll probably find most are anxiety-driven. That's worth knowing.

---
## EXERCISE v15
Tell someone the number. Step 1: Pick one person you trust. Step 2: Tell them your savings, your fear, your actual situation. Step 3: Usually: they don't judge. The anxiety thrives in secrecy. Disclosure breaks the cycle.

---
## EXERCISE v16
Do one thing with money without analysis. Step 1: Spend on something small, something you want, without calculating impact. Step 2: Feel the discomfort. Step 3: You're practicing: money can be spent without catastrophe. It's a practice. You're learning it.

---
## EXERCISE v17
Stop comparing. Step 1: Next time you find yourself calculating against someone else's finances, notice it. Step 2: Ask: does this help? Step 3: It doesn't. Comparison is anxiety fuel. Don't feed it.

---
## EXERCISE v18
Create a real contingency plan. Step 1: Not in your head. Written down. What would actually happen if you lost your job? Step 2: Usually you'll find: something. Savings, family, unemployment, skills. Step 3: The plan is usually less scary than the undefined worry.

---
## EXERCISE v19
Notice the difference between fear and fact. Step 1: This month, track: fears about money and facts about money separately. Step 2: At the end, compare. Step 3: The gap shows you: most of the anxiety is about things that aren't happening.

---
## EXERCISE v20
Practice "I don't know and I'm okay." Step 1: The future is genuinely uncertain. Step 2: Practice saying: I don't know what the market will do, and I'm okay. Step 3: Accepting uncertainty is the antidote to anxiety. Not certainty. Acceptance.

---
## EXERCISE v21
Turn off notifications for one account. Step 1: Pick one financial account. Step 2: Disable alerts and monitoring for one month. Step 3: What happens? Usually: nothing. That's important. The monitoring wasn't protective.

---
## EXERCISE v22
Trace the anxiety to its source. Step 1: When did you become anxious about money? Step 2: What was happening then? Step 3: Usually there's a story: something happened, someone taught you to worry, something was lost. Name it. That context shifts things.

---
## EXERCISE v23
Stop planning for every contingency. Step 1: What are you planning for that probably won't happen? Step 2: Release one scenario from your planning. Step 3: You're practicing: not all possible futures need to be prepared for. Some you just accept when they come.

---
## EXERCISE v24
Do the vulnerability work. Step 1: Write: I'm afraid I'll become poor. Step 2: Write: What would be true about me if I were poor? Step 3: Usually: you'd still be you. You'd still have skills, worth, capacity. The fear is about identity loss, not money loss.

---
## EXERCISE v25
Create a actual rest budget. Step 1: Just like you budget money, budget rest/joy. Step 2: This comes before savings optimization. This comes before security. Step 3: You're practicing: rest is not a luxury after you achieve safety. It's necessary now.

---
## EXERCISE v26
Notice when you're using financial control as emotional regulation. Step 1: When you're anxious, do you go check accounts? Step 2: That's self-soothing through false control. Step 3: Find a different soothing method. The accounts aren't actually soothing anything.

---
## EXERCISE v27
Ask what you're sacrificing for the security. Step 1: What have you stopped doing to save money? Step 2: What joy have you deferred? Step 3: Is the security you're building more valuable than the life you're living? That's a real question.

---
## EXERCISE v28
Challenge one belief per week. Step 1: Pick one financial belief you hold (safety requires x amount, security requires perfect tracking, etc.). Step 2: Write evidence for and against. Step 3: You'll find the belief isn't as solid as it feels. That loosens its grip.

---
## EXERCISE v29
Do the opposite of what anxiety suggests. Step 1: What does financial anxiety want you to do? Probably: save more, track more, earn more. Step 2: Do the opposite: spend something, stop tracking for a day, rest. Step 3: You're showing your nervous system: you can survive the thing it fears.

---
## EXERCISE v30
Name the life you're building with all this security. Step 1: If you achieve financial security, what happens next? Step 2: Usually there's no answer. Security becomes the goal instead of security enabling something. Step 3: Define what you're actually building toward. That clarity changes the calculation.
