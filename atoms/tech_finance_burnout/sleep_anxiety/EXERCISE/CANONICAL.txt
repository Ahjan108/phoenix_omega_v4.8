## EXERCISE v01
The System Shutdown Protocol. Step 1: At 9pm, begin an explicit shutdown of work systems. Close all work applications. Close email. Close Slack. Not just backgrounding—actually close them. Step 2: Spend 5 minutes documenting what's running: any jobs, processes, pending items. Write it down. Step 3: Set a specific time you'll check on systems (not before 7am tomorrow). Step 4: Tell yourself: "I've handed off responsibility for now. Monitoring will resume at X time." Step 5: This creates a boundary between work-on and work-off. Sleep can happen in work-off.

## EXERCISE v02
The Threat Assessment Reality Check. Step 1: Identify the threat your system is detecting at night. Step 2: Ask: "Is this threat actionable right now?" Usually no. Step 3: Ask: "What's the earliest time this threat becomes actionable?" Write it down. Step 4: This creates a temporal boundary. The threat is real but it's not right-now. Step 5: You can tell your nervous system: "I'll address this at [time]. For now, it's not actionable."

---
## EXERCISE v03
The Information Sufficiency Check. Step 1: You're anxious about information you don't have. Step 2: Ask: "What information would make me feel better?" Step 3: Ask: "Is that information available to me right now?" Almost never. Step 4: Ask: "When will that information be available?" Usually business hours. Step 5: The anxiety is about information that will exist tomorrow but doesn't exist tonight. The anxiety is premature. You can tell your system: "The information will exist tomorrow. Tonight, I rest."

---
## EXERCISE v04
The Compartmentalization Container. Step 1: Use a physical or digital container to hold work items. This could be a notebook, a note in an app, literally a box. Step 2: Before bed, put everything you're anxious about into the container. Don't solve it, just capture it. Step 3: The act of putting it somewhere tells your brain: this is contained. It's not running loose in my head. Step 4: Sleep knowing the items are captured. They won't be lost. You'll address them tomorrow. Step 5: The containment is often more important than the resolution.

---
## EXERCISE v05
The Nervous System Reset. Step 1: At 8pm, before the anxiety spiral, do something that's incompatible with threat detection. Not work (that activates threat), not screens (that activates threat). Step 2: Maybe a walk, a bath, stretching, cooking something. Something that requires presence. Step 3: The goal is to shift your nervous system out of threat-detection mode. Step 4: Do this consistently. Your body learns: evening = non-threat state. Step 5: Consistent evening practices train your nervous system into the state you want for sleep.

## EXERCISE v06
The Market Simulation Limit. Step 1: You're probably running scenarios about market conditions. Set a specific time window when you're allowed to do this: maybe 7-8pm. Step 2: After that window, scenarios are off-limits. Step 3: When a scenario arrives after hours, note it: "Market simulation. Available after 7pm tomorrow." Step 4: The limitation is important. You can't scenario-endlessly and also sleep. Step 5: You're training yourself to think about work during work-time, not all-the-time.

## EXERCISE v07
The Sleep Expectation Reset. Step 1: You're probably expecting perfect sleep. You're probably also not getting it. Step 2: Reset the expectation: "I'll sleep okay tonight. Not perfectly, but okay." Step 3: "Okay" is 6 hours, fitful, waking in the middle. That's still acceptable. Step 4: The lower expectation often leads to better sleep because the pressure drops. Step 5: Sleep better by expecting less.

## EXERCISE v08
The Actionability Timeline. Step 1: Identify something you're anxious about. Step 2: Ask: "Can I take action on this in the next 4 hours?" If yes, act now or at specific time tomorrow morning. Step 3: If no, the anxiety is premature. Step 4: Create a specific time you'll address it tomorrow. Step 5: You're moving the anxiety out of night-time and into a specific day-time slot where you have agency.

---
## EXERCISE v09
The Cognitive Distancing. Step 1: When an anxious thought arrives at night, label it: "This is a thought my system is generating. It's not necessarily true." Step 2: The labeling creates distance between you and the thought. Step 3: You're not dismissing the thought. You're observing it as a thought rather than absorbing it as reality. Step 4: Practice this distinction: the thought exists. The threat doesn't necessarily exist. Step 5: Over time, this distance reduces the thought's power.

---
## EXERCISE v10
The Ritual Transition. Step 1: Create a specific bedtime ritual that signals to your system: work-time is over, rest-time is beginning. Step 2: It could be: warm shower, change clothes, specific music, journaling 3 thoughts. Step 3: The ritual has to be the same every night. The consistency is what trains your nervous system. Step 4: Your body recognizes the ritual and starts downshifting. Step 5: A 15-minute ritual can be more powerful than hours of trying to fall asleep.

## EXERCISE v11
The System Monitoring Schedule. Step 1: Instead of constant monitoring (which keeps you activated), schedule specific check-in times. Step 2: Maybe 7am, 12pm, 5pm, 8pm. Step 3: Outside those times, don't check. Step 4: At night, you can tell yourself: "Check-in is at 7am. I'll know then." Step 5: The scheduled monitoring is often sufficient. The continuous monitoring is what activates the threat detection.

## EXERCISE v12
The Resource Inventory. Step 1: You're anxious because you feel unprepared for what might happen. Step 2: Make a list of resources you actually have: skills, money, support, experience. Step 3: Make a list of what you'd do if the feared scenario happened. Step 4: Usually, you have more resources and more options than the anxiety suggests. Step 5: The inventory is grounding. It reminds you that you have agency.

## EXERCISE v13
The Sleep Trade-off Analysis. Step 1: You're losing sleep to gain a sense of control through continued work-thinking. Step 2: What's the actual trade-off? Sleep quality and time for health and cognitive function. Step 3: Is the control worth the cost? Usually not. Step 4: What if you accepted the loss of control in exchange for sleep? What would you lose? Usually very little. Step 5: The analysis often clarifies the irrationality of the trade you're making.

## EXERCISE v14
The Pre-Sleep Review. Step 1: At 9pm, spend 10 minutes reviewing: what got done today? What's left? What actually matters? Step 2: The review is brief and non-action oriented. Step 3: You're not solving anything. You're creating closure on the day. Step 4: Once reviewed, it's done. You can sleep. Step 5: Without the review, your brain keeps trying to complete the day. The review gives it closure.

## EXERCISE v15
The Confidence Anchoring. Step 1: Before bed, write down 3 things you've successfully handled. Not perfect outcomes, just successful handling of complexity. Step 2: Your system needs evidence that you can handle hard things. Step 3: Read these 3 things before sleep. Step 4: Tell yourself: "I've handled complexity before. I can handle whatever comes." Step 5: The anchoring builds confidence that the anxiety is borrowing without permission.

## EXERCISE v16
The Breath Pattern. Step 1: Use a specific breathing pattern to signal rest to your nervous system. Maybe: inhale for 4, hold for 4, exhale for 6. Step 2: The extended exhale signals parasympathetic activation. Step 3: Do this for 5 minutes before sleep. Step 4: Do it consistently. Your system learns: this breathing = rest time. Step 5: Physiology before psychology. The breathing changes the state.

## EXERCISE v17
The Context Bracketing. Step 1: Work contexts and sleep contexts should be separate. Step 2: If you work in your bedroom, you've merged the contexts. Separate them if possible. Step 3: If separation isn't possible, use a transition: close the laptop, change location, change clothes. Step 4: The physical separation helps your mind separate. Step 5: Your brain needs to know: this place/time is work. This place/time is sleep.

---
## EXERCISE v18
The Worst-Case Survival Plan. Step 1: The thing you're anxious about: trace it to the worst possible outcome. Step 2: If that happened, what would you do? Write it down. Step 3: You can usually figure out what you'd do. That figuring-it-out is reassuring. Step 4: Once you know you'd survive it, the immediate threat level drops. Step 5: The system often activates because it's convinced you can't handle the worst case. Prove to it that you can.

## EXERCISE v19
The Boundary Communication. Step 1: Tell someone (or yourself) explicitly: "I'm not available for work-related thinking after 9pm." Step 2: Make it real. Set an autoresponder if needed. Step 3: The boundary creates permission. You're not neglecting work. You're managing yourself. Step 4: Consistency on the boundary trains your system to downshift at 9pm. Step 5: External boundaries often work better than internal willpower.

---
## EXERCISE v20
The Completion Ritual. Step 1: At the end of the work day, do something that signals completion. Not "I'm done working." But "The work day is complete." Step 2: It could be: close all tabs, write "day complete" in a journal, specific phrase. Step 3: This creates psychological completion even if things are unfinished. Step 4: The completion signal helps your system move from work-mode to rest-mode. Step 5: Without the signal, your system thinks work is still ongoing. The ritual ends the ongoing-ness.

## EXERCISE v21
The Permission to Unfinish. Step 1: You're probably anxious because things are unfinished. Problems to solve, code to review, emails to answer. Step 2: Give yourself explicit permission: "These things will remain unfinished tonight. That's okay." Step 3: Write it down if needed. Step 4: The permission often reduces the anxiety more than the finishing would. Step 5: Your system is anxious about the unfinished state. Permission to unfinish can calm that.

## EXERCISE v22
The External Processing. Step 1: You're probably processing work-anxiety alone in your head. Step 2: Talk to someone. Not to solve it but to process it. Verbalizing often changes the intensity. Step 3: Someone outside the work context can provide perspective you can't. Step 4: The talking itself is a circuit-breaker for the loop. Step 5: You don't have to carry this alone.

## EXERCISE v23
The Sleep Debt Perspective. Step 1: View sleep not as nightly achievement but as weekly rhythm. Step 2: Some nights are bad. Some are good. Over the week, you accumulate. Step 3: This removes pressure from tonight. One bad night doesn't determine the week. Step 4: You're in a rhythm, not a daily pass-fail. Step 5: The rhythm perspective reduces the urgency of perfecting each night.

## EXERCISE v24
The Hypervigilance Naming. Step 1: You're in a state of hypervigilance. Name it. "My system is in hypervigilance." Step 2: Hypervigilance is useful in emergencies. It's exhausting in non-emergencies. Step 3: Ask: "Is there actually an emergency right now?" Usually no. Step 4: Tell your system: "Emergency mode is not needed right now. We're safe." Step 5: Naming and contradicting the hypervigilance can start to recalibrate it.

## EXERCISE v25
The Responsibility Boundaries. Step 1: Write down what you're actually responsible for versus what you're anxious about. Step 2: Most anxiety is for things outside your actual responsibility. Step 3: You're carrying extra responsibility through anxiety. Step 4: Write: "I am responsible for X. The rest is not my responsibility." Step 5: Clarity on boundaries reduces the load you're carrying.

## EXERCISE v26
The Thought Noting. Step 1: Don't try to stop the thoughts. Instead, as anxious thoughts arrive, note them: "Market thought," "Code thought," "Risk thought." Step 2: The noting creates distance and acknowledges the thought without engaging it. Step 3: You're training: let the thought come and go. Don't grab it. Step 4: Over time, thoughts come but don't stick because they're not being engaged. Step 5: Noting is more effective than fighting.

---
## EXERCISE v27
The Nervous System Awareness. Step 1: Notice what activates your threat detection: certain news, certain metrics, certain messages. Step 2: Reduce exposure to activators in the evening. Step 3: You can't eliminate activators. But you can choose not to encounter them before bed. Step 4: Awareness of what activates you is the first step to managing it. Step 5: You have more control over exposure than you think.

---
## EXERCISE v28
The Micro-Reset. Step 1: If you wake up at 3am anxious, don't try to force sleep. Step 2: Get up. Do something for 15 minutes. Not work. Something gentle. Step 3: Return to bed with a different state. Step 4: The state change is sometimes what's needed to break the anxiety loop. Step 5: Forcing sleep when you're activated makes it worse. Reset and try again.

---
## EXERCISE v29
The Value Clarification. Step 1: Why do you do this work? What matters about it? Step 2: Usually it's not the metrics or the immediate outcomes. It's something deeper. Step 3: Remember why it matters. Step 4: When anxiety arrives at night, reconnect with the why. Step 5: The why is more grounding than the what-ifs.

---
## EXERCISE v30
The Acceptance of Uncertainty. Step 1: The core of the sleep anxiety is often: discomfort with uncertainty. Step 2: You want to know how things will go. You can't. Step 3: Practice small uncertainties: send something without re-reviewing. Delegate something. Make a quick decision. Step 4: Survive the small uncertainties. Your system learns: uncertainty is survivable. Step 5: The practice during the day changes the nervous system's response at night.
