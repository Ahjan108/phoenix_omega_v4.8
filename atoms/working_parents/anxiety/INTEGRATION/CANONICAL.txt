## INTEGRATION v01 | body_grounded_still_anxious
---
integration_mode: BODY-LANDED
reframe_type: BODY-FACT
---
I'm sitting at the kitchen counter with my feet on the floor. The tile is cool. My hands are flat on the counter in front of me, and they're not shaking right now. I can feel that. Five minutes ago I was calculating the pediatrician cost and the school payment and whether there's enough before Friday. My chest was tight. Now it's just tight. Not closing. Just tight, like a held breath.

I used to think the anxiety would go away if I fixed enough things. If I got ahead. If I had enough buffer. But sitting here with my feet on the floor and my hands visible, I realize this is the anxiety—it doesn't leave. It gets smaller. It gets older. It becomes the background hum instead of the emergency siren.

My body knows the difference now. My body can feel tight and still be capable. My hands can shake at 8 AM and still make breakfast. My chest can be a knot and my child can still get to school.

Carry: My body is both anxious and capable.

## INTEGRATION v02 | night_witness_spiral_stops
---
integration_mode: BODY-LANDED
reframe_type: SPIRAL-INTERRUPT
---
It's 11:47 PM and I've been awake for an hour cycling through: Did I respond to that email properly. Will the school say something tomorrow. Am I doing this parenting thing wrong. What if the car breaks down. What if I lose my job.

But tonight something is different. I noticed the spiral happening. I wasn't inside it the whole time; I watched it from somewhere slightly outside. Like I could see the thought appear and then the next thought and recognize they were thoughts, not reality.

My heart is still racing. Sleep still won't come. But my hands stopped gripping the sheets ten minutes ago. My jaw unclenched without me forcing it. My body remembered something: spiraling doesn't require my full participation. I can witness it and be separate from it.

This won't last forever. Tomorrow the anxiety might own me completely again. But for tonight, I'm here. Separate. Watching. And that's a kind of relief I didn't expect.

Carry: I can watch the spiral without living inside it.

## INTEGRATION v03 | ground_in_one_thing
---
integration_mode: BODY-LANDED
reframe_type: SENSORY-FOCUS
---
The coffee is hot in my hands. That's the thing I'm holding right now. Not the three calls I need to make or the permission slip I forgot or the conversation with my partner about money. Just this cup. The heat is real. The weight is real.

I learned this in the car this morning. The anxiety was climbing—the calendar is too full, the week is impossible, I can't fit it all—and then my hands touched the steering wheel and I remembered: I can only drive this car right now. Not next week. Not next month. This car. This second.

My nervous system doesn't know the difference between the future anxiety and the present moment, but my hands do. My hands know what's real. Hot coffee. Steering wheel. The door handle when I'm holding it.

My mind is still generating seventeen worried thoughts. But my hands are holding one warm thing.

When this settles—and it will, temporarily—I'll remember that the body is slower than the mind. The body moves in present tense. The body is right.

Carry: My hands know what's real right now.

## INTEGRATION v04 | anxiety_as_signal_not_emergency
---
integration_mode: BODY-LANDED
reframe_type: SIGNAL-REFRAME
---
My shoulders are at my ears right now. Have been for days. When I notice them there and drop them, it feels like failure—like I wasn't managing the anxiety, I was just hiding from it. But today I realized: my shoulders being at my ears is information. It means something is asking for attention.

Not "I'm broken." Not "I'm failing at calmness." Just: something is alerting. My child is sick. The deadline moved. The bank account is lower than expected. There's actual information my body is sending. The anxiety isn't the problem. The information is.

When I drop my shoulders without addressing what they're signaling, they just climb back up. But when I ask them what they know, when I listen to the information—oh, the body relax differently. Not because I'm "doing calm" but because I've received the message.

My shoulders are still high. But now I know what they're saying. And I know I get to decide what to do about it, not panic about the panic.

Carry: My anxiety is information, not failure.

## INTEGRATION v05 | rest_between_spirals_counts
---
integration_mode: BODY-LANDED
reframe_type: MICRO-REST
---
I had twenty minutes today where the anxiety wasn't active. I was folding laundry and my mind wasn't generating worst-case scenarios. I was just folding. The folded shirts were accumulating in the basket. That was the whole world.

I didn't notice it until it was over. That's how quiet it was. I've spent so long waiting for the anxiety to be gone that I miss the moments when it's just... absent. When my body isn't bracing. When my breath is just breath.

Twenty minutes isn't healing. It's not a cure. It won't last. But it proves something: the anxiety isn't permanent. The body can remember neutral. The nervous system can remember that nothing is happening right now.

I'm not trying to extend that twenty minutes into something bigger. I'm just noting: it happened. It's possible. My body is capable of not-anxious, even if only in small pockets.

When the next spiral comes—and it will—I'll know that between spirals there are minutes where I'm just a person folding clothes.

Carry: Even twenty minutes of calm is real.

## INTEGRATION v06 | physical_action_interrupts_thought
---
integration_mode: BODY-LANDED
reframe_type: ACTION-INTERRUPT
---
This morning the anxiety was taking over before my feet hit the floor. What-ifs were already queuing up. But I got out of bed, put my feet on the cold floor, and the cold was loud enough that the what-ifs had to pause.

I ran cold water on my wrists. I opened the bathroom window. I let the cool air hit my face.

The anxiety didn't go away. It's still here. But my nervous system registered that something changed. Something physical occurred. The body doesn't distinguish between "I took an action" and "I am safe." It just knows: something moved.

I've been taught that I should think my way out of anxiety. Find the logical argument against the worried thought. Reason with myself. But my body isn't interested in logic right now. My body wants evidence that I'm still here, still functioning, still capable of physical action.

Cold water is physical evidence. Movement is physical evidence. The fact that I can still operate my body even while anxious—that's the thing that settles my nervous system.

Not "the anxiety is gone." Just "I'm still here."

Carry: Movement proves I'm still functioning.

## INTEGRATION v07 | naming_normalizes_presence
---
integration_mode: BODY-LANDED
reframe_type: WITNESS-NAME
---
Today I said to my partner: "I'm anxious right now." Out loud. Not hiding it in my body. Not pretending I'm fine. Just stating it like weather.

My partner couldn't fix it. There was nothing to fix. But when I named it, something in my body relaxed. The anxiety was still there—I could feel it in my shoulders, my stomach, my chest. But it wasn't secret. It wasn't something I was failing to manage alone.

My partner said, "Okay," and kept cooking dinner. The world didn't end. The anxiety didn't get bigger because I admitted it.

Naming takes the energy out of hiding. Instead of using half my capacity to contain the anxiety, I can use full capacity to just... exist with it. It's still uncomfortable. I'm still tense. But I'm not alone with it in my head.

My body could feel the difference. When I said the words, my hands unclenched. Not much. Just slightly. Like I'd been holding something and finally set it down.

The anxiety is still living in my shoulders. But now my partner knows. Now it's not the thing I'm managing in secret.

Carry: Naming the anxiety makes it smaller.
